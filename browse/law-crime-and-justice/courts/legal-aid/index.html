Overview

If you can't afford to pay for a lawyer, you can apply for legal aid.

Legal aid is government funding to pay for legal help if you can’t afford a lawyer. You may have to pay back some or all of the legal aid you get. There are different types available.

Legal aid for criminal offences

Legal aid for family or civil disputes

Who can get legal aid

If you can't afford a lawyer, you can apply for legal aid for criminal court proceedings, civil disputes or family disputes. You can also apply if you're a victim of a violent offence and want to apply for a non-contact order.

Non-contact orders

To decide whether you can afford a lawyer, Legal Aid Services will consider:

  • how much you earn before tax
  • the value of your assets, such as house and car

how many financially dependent children you have.
If you have a partner, their finances will also be considered.

If you've been charged with a criminal offence, they'll also look at whether you're:

  • charged with an offence that could be punished with a prison term of six months or more
  • appealing your conviction or sentence
  • appearing before the parole board.

Who can get criminal legal aid

If the charges are minor

If you face minor charges, you might not need to apply for legal aid to get free help.

At your district court reception, ask to see the duty lawyer.

The duty lawyer can help you through your first day in court and will be able to tell you if you need to hire another lawyer. The duty lawyer can help you apply for legal aid.

Legal help on your first day in court

If it's a family or civil case

If you're taking a family or civil case to court, you'll need to appoint your own lawyer, who will talk to you about applying for legal aid.

If you're the victim of a violent offence

If you're a victim of a violent offence and you want to apply for a non-contact order, you can apply for civil legal aid to help you do it.

The offender can also apply for civil legal aid to assist in responding to the non-contact order.

Non-contact orders

How to apply

For family or civil disputes, your lawyer will help you apply.

For criminal legal aid, you need to fill in an application form. You can get the application form (and free help to complete it) from:

  • the duty lawyer at your district court (ask at the reception desk)
  • your local legal aid office
  • a Community Law Centre.

Contact legal aid

Find a Community Law Centre

Apply as soon as you've been charged or received a summons to appear in court. Don't wait until your first day in court.

Documents you’ll need

If you've been charged with an offence

To apply for criminal legal aid you'll need to provide:

  • your court summons
  • your address and phone numbers
  • your date of birth
  • the place where your case is being heard and your next court date
  • your income for the last 12 months (include wages, benefits, ACC payments)
  • details of any savings or debt you have (such as hire-purchase payments)
  • details of any major assets you own (such as a house or car)
  • how many financially dependent children you have.

If you're in a civil or family dispute

To apply for family or civil legal aid you'll need to provide:

  • your address and phone numbers
  • your date of birth
  • your income for the last 12 months (include wages, benefits, ACC payments)
  • details of any savings or debt you have (such as hire-purchase payments)
  • details of any major assets you own (such as a house or car)
  • how many financially dependent children you have
  • your partner’s financial situation
  • any letters or documents to do with your case.

What it costs

You may have to pay back some or all of the legal aid you get. Your lawyer will explain this to you.

Paying back legal aid

What happens next

After you've applied, you'll get a letter telling you whether legal aid has been granted.

If you're refused legal aid, you can ask the managing legal aid office to review the legal aid decision.

Asking for a review of a legal aid decision

Less serious charges — you're assigned a lawyer

The letter telling you that legal aid is granted will include the name of the lawyer assigned to you and their contact details.

You must make the first contact with your lawyer. You must try to make an appointment with them before your first court appearance or before you go back to court.

If you're already receiving legal aid for another criminal court case, the lawyer who has been representing you will usually continue to represent you on any new charges.

Very serious charges — you can choose a lawyer

If you face very serious criminal charges and may go to prison for more than 10 years, you can choose a lawyer to represent you. The lawyer will need to be an approved legal aid provider.

Some organisations can help you find legal aid, or you can choose a lawyer from recommendations of friends, family or other people you trust.

Other free legal help

Community law centres

Community law centres offer free legal information and advice to people who can't afford to see a lawyer privately.

Find a community law centre

If you're eligible, community law centres may also be able to:

  • offer ongoing legal assistance
  • help you with mediation
  • represent you at tribunals or in court.

Citizens advice bureau

Free advice about just about anything — in your local area and in your own language.
