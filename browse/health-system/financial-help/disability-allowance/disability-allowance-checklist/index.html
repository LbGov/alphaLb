Disability Allowance checklist

Most people talk to their doctor or health practitioner about what they need before they apply for the Disability Allowance. This checklist explains the help you’re entitled to.

You only get an allowance for the items you claim for. If you leave something out, you have to apply again to get it.

If you’re unsure if your expenses count, talk to your doctor or health practitioner. You can also contact Work and Income to ask for advice.

Disability Allowance

Health and medical costs

You can claim for:

  • extra doctors’, specialists’ and hospital fees you have to pay because of your disability, eg physiotherapy, audiology, podiatry or dental services
  • prescription charges for subsidised medicines you use regularly
  • rental and monitoring of a medical alarm, including the cost of the phone connection, if your life or health would be at risk, or your disability would be worse without it
  • special foods if you, and your doctor or dietician agree that you need them to manage your disability, eg vitamins, supplements, herbal remedies and minerals
  • counselling fees and transport for up to 10 sessions, or more if clinically necessary
  • other health treatments if they are approved by a doctor, and provided or supervised by a registered health practitioner, eg a chiropractor, osteopath or acupuncturist.

Personal medical alarms

Household costs

You can claim for:

  • getting your gardening and lawns done
  • having the outside of your windows cleaned
  • help with your housework, if you have a Community Services Card
  • the cost of power, gas and heating, if your disability or illness means your bills are higher than for a household of the same size.

Community Services Card​

Personal supplies and equipment

You can claim for:

  • supplies that you need to manage your disability, including
  • batteries for your hearing aids
  • vet's fees for guide dogs
  • incontinence products
  • hiring a wheelchair.
  • clothes or shoes that are made-to-measure or wear out more quickly because of your disability, eg from using a wheelchair or an artificial limb.
  • the cost of day care, if you’re 65 or older.

Transport

You can claim for:

  • transport to regular health appointments for treatment of your disability, eg travel to your doctor or a counsellor
  • extra travel costs of a one-off visit to a doctor, hospital, specialist or counsellor
  • the costs of getting to and from day care if you’re 65 years or older
  • other travel you have to pay for — this can include everyday activities such as shopping, job training and getting to work.
  • If your partner is in a rest home or in hospital for more than 12 weeks, the Special Disability Allowance can help with the cost of visiting them.
