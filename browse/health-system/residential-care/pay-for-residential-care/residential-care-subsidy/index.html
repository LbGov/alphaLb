What you get

The Residential Care Subsidy helps with the cost of your long-term care and is paid directly to your rest home or hospital. You apply to Work and Income.

The subsidy you get depends on your income and assets. It is the difference between what you can contribute and the cost of the care provided by the government.

The biggest subsidy — called the maximum contribution — pays for the full cost of services which your rest home or hospital provides under their contract with the local District Health Board (DHB).

The amount of the maximum contribution depends on the area you live in. It is a capped amount that ranges from $973.91 to $1062.95 per week including GST.

Maximum contribution from 1 July 2017 

You’re responsible for the cost of your care while you’re waiting for the outcome of your application. If it’s approved, payments can be back-dated for up to 90 days from the date your application was received by Work and Income.

If you already get a NZ government pension or benefit

If you receive NZ Super, a Veterans’ Pension or another benefit most of this goes towards the cost of your care in a rest home or hospital.

If you get NZ Super or a Veterans’ Pension, you keep a personal allowance of $43.93 per week. You’ll also get an annual clothing allowance of $275.50 each April. The government reviews these allowances from time to time.

Who can get it

You can apply for a Residential Care Subsidy if:

  • you’re a New Zealand citizen or resident 
  • a needs assessment confirms you need long-term residential care, and
  • you’re moving to, or living in a rest home or hospital that has a contract for your care with the local District Health Board (DHB).

You must be:

  • 65 years or older, and not have income and assets that could cover the cost of your care, or
  • aged 50–64, single and have no dependent children.

You may not be eligible if you’re 65 or older, and:

  • your assets are more than $224,654, including your house and car
  • your assets are more than $123,025, excluding your house and car.

If your partner still lives in your joint home, you can choose whether or not to include your house and car when you calculate your assets.

Who can't apply

You can’t apply if you’re:

  • under 65 and have a partner or dependent children
  • under 50 and single.

If you’re under 50 and have major health or disability problems, you may be able to get a Residential Support Subsidy to move into residential care. Talk to your doctor or healthcare team for further advice.

Residential Support Subsidy

Before you apply

Before you apply you must get a needs assessment through the Needs Assessment Coordination Service (NASC) at your District Health Board (DHB). The assessment must confirm that you need residential care.

Getting a needs assessment

How to apply — 65 years or older

If you’ve been assessed as needing residential care, you need to tell Work and Income about your assets and income so they can decide if you’re eligible for the Residential Care Subsidy.

Ask the Needs Assessment Service Coordination (NASC) assessor for a Residential Care Subsidy Financial Means Assessment form.

  1. Fill out the form.
  2. Make a copy of your completed form and any supporting documents before you send them.
  3. Send it to the Residential Subsidy Unit (at Work and Income) within 90 days of the date you want to start receiving the subsidy.

Submit your application as soon as possible, even if it is not fully completed. The subsidy payments can only be back-dated to 90 days from the date Work and Income receive your application. You pay the full cost of your care for any time you’re in residential care before that 90 days.

About the financial means assessment

The government sets maximum levels for assets and income. If you’re at or below these levels, you’re eligible for a Residential Care Subsidy. The actual amount you get depends on the type of care you need and how much you can contribute from your income.

Work and Income looks at your assets first. If they are at or below the level, Work and Income then look at your income.

You must provide information about:

  • your assets such as property, savings, investments, loans, boats or campervans
  • your net income (ie after tax) from all sources at the date of your application, including superannuation.

If you give away or sell assets for less than they are worth, Work and Income may decide to include them when they assess your application.

Working out your assets

If you’re single or your partner is already in care, to get a Residential Care Subsidy your assets must be $224,654 or less.

If your partner is still living in your joint home, there are 2 options for working out your assets.

  • Your joint assets must be $123,025 or less, not including your car and the home your partner lives in.
  • Your joint assets must be $224,654 or less, including your car and the home your partner lives in.

Help for partners staying at home

Some assets aren’t included in the assessment, for example:

  • your home and car if your partner or dependent children are using these
  • up to $10,000 for a funeral plan recognised by Work and Income, ie a plan that only lets you use the money for your funeral
  • any lump sum payment you received from the Accident Compensation Corporation (ACC) in the previous 12 months
  • personal belongings including jewellery and family heirlooms or taonga
  • your household contents, including appliances and furniture.

Assets exempt from a means assessment

Debts

If you have debts, you should pay them off before you apply. In some circumstances you may be able to deduct them from your assets.

Working out your income

Income includes:

  • government benefits, including NZ Super and the Veteran's Pension
  • 50% of any private pension
  • 50% of life insurance payments
  • investments such as Kiwisaver, shares or rent
  • income from a family trust or estate.

Some income is exempt, including:

  • income your partner earns from employment
  • some payments from ACC, and
  • payments made as a result of the Canterbury earthquakes.

Income excluded from a means assessment

Family trusts

If you have a family trust or another type of trust, Work and Income may ask you to supply more information about your involvement.

Gifts of money or assets

If you’ve given any gifts with a total value of $6,000 or more per year in the 5 years before you apply, you must include these in your assets.

Before those 5 years, you can give away up to $27,000 per year without having to declare it as an asset. If you have a partner, these amounts are shared, not doubled.

Work and Income can also ask you about any other gift you’ve made before those 5 years if they think it should be included in your assessment.

The rules about gifting when you apply for the Residential Care Subsidy are different from other government rules about gifts, eg Inland Revenue’s rules.

Gifts in recognition of care

If someone has been caring for you for more than 12 months, any money you’ve gifted them up to $6,000 per year can be excluded from your assets. It depends on your circumstances, but they usually need to be:

  • living with you, but not a partner or dependent child
  • not receiving any government funding to care for you
  • giving a high level of personal care, eg helping you with bathing, dressing or getting to the toilet.

Without their help you would already have moved into residential care.

Gifting in recognition of care

How to apply — 50 to 64 years

If you’ve been assessed as needing residential care, you need to tell Work and Income about your income so they can decide if you’re eligible for the Residential Care Subsidy.

  1. Ask the NASC assessor for a Residential Care Subsidy Application for Financial Means Assessment form.
  2. Fill out the form.
  3. Return it to the Residential Subsidy Unit at Work and Income.

Payments can be back-dated more than 90 days.

When you find out

Your application usually takes about 4 weeks to process. It can take longer than this if Work and Income needs more information or needs to investigate your financial situation further.

What happens next

Work and Income writes to tell you the result of your financial means assessment. Work and Income also tells the Ministry of Health (MoH), your local District Health Board (DHB) and the rest home or hospital you’ve moved to that your subsidy has been approved. The subsidy is paid to the rest home by the MoH.

If you’ve been paying for your care, the rest home or hospital refunds your payments that are covered by the Residential Care Subsidy for up to 90 days before the date of your application.

Depending on your income, you may still have to pay part of the cost of your care.

If you can’t get a Residential Care Subsidy

If your assets and income are too high, then you must pay for your own care. You pay:

  • the maximum contribution for the services your rest home provides under its contract with the DHB, and
  • for any other services you’ve agreed that the rest home will provide for you.

Paying for residential care

If you own your own home, Work and Income may suggest that you arrange a Residential Care Loan to help with the costs of your rest-home care.

Residential Care Loan

Getting your application reviewed

If your assets or income have reduced since you applied, you can ask for a review of your financial means assessment.

Contact the Residential Subsidy Unit at Work and Income. They'll tell you what information they need and whether you need to fill out any forms.

Freephone: 0800 999 727.

When you no longer need the subsidy

The rest home or hospital advises Work and Income:

  • if you move out of residential care, or
  • in the event of your death.

Someone acting for you can also advise Work and Income.

In both cases your Residential Care Subsidy payments end.

Related financial help

If you own your own home, a Residential Care Loan can help you meet the costs of residential care.
