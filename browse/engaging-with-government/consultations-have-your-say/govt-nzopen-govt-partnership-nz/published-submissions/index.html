I believe the theme of the second National Action Plan for Open Government should be Open data, and not just open as in the ability to access that data but open also in a citizen’s ability to reuse that data.

Submitted on 09/08/2016 4:18pm

How could we improve on New Zealand’s last National Action Plan?

While I applaud the effort going into practical projects, it is not possible for initiatives like these to deliver the scale of change envisaged.  That scale can only come if the majority of individuals working government understand and share the vision and understand how they are empowered to contribute to that vision.  This requires the following:

- A clear set of short, pithy principles for open government

- A comprehensive programme to share and explain those principles and the vision they enable, to all staff within government.

- Support services for staff who wish to act to deliver those principles but are unclear on their powers and the practicalities of how to use them.

- Identification and promotion of cultural change ambassadors who have gone through the process above and can share their knowledge and experience with colleagues.

What does open government mean to you in practice?

It means a deep seated culture that recognises, in the immortal words of Abraham Lincoln, that government is "of the people, by the people, for the people" and as a result the Government can only exclude the general public under very specific and well documented conditions.

What do you think should be the themes of the second National Action Plan for Open Government should be? e.g. Open data, engagement, open budgets?

1.  Cultural change across all government employees as set out above because the only way to achieve the scale of change envisaged is if everyone in government actively participates to make it happen.

2.  Open data, which is the most effective means of enabling a scrutiny role by citizens.  Specifically, by providing open data, control over the areas of scrutiny is lost and people are free to find entirely new areas to scrutinise - innovation in the scrutiny process.

3.  Transparency around the evidence and opinions provided to decision makers for major decisions, to enable scrutiny of that evidence and those opinions.  In the words of Louis Brandeis, “sunlight is said to be the best of disinfectants”, which means both removing unsubstantiated evidence or poorly researched opinion and the positive effect of increasing confidence when evidence and opinion are of high quality.

Submitted on 19/08/2016 3:27pm

How could we improve on New Zealand’s last National Action Plan?

i) First and fundamentally, by starting from the right place – recognising that the OGP is NOT about the absolute levels of transparency, participation etc in each member country, it is about the DELTA, it is about improving where each country is, no matter what its starting point.

This was one of the core parts of the OGP mechanism that President Obama set up. It is why the other co-founder of the OGP (Brazil) was a ‘southern country’, to distinguish the OGP from many international ‘clubs’ where the advanced countries tell the rest what they need to do.  NZ got off on the wrong foot with a self-congratulatory approach based on trumpeting how transparent we already are and how wonderful Better Public Services is - without adding any additional ‘stretch’ beyond existing BPS policy, or consulting the public in a meaningful way on the Action Plan that reflected OGP principles. It then adopted a defensive approach when its Action Plan was not well-received. And the way this draft Introductory paper is written continues to reflect that failure to understand what the OGP is fundamentally about.

ii) By genuine consultation and co-creation. Genuine openness would mean that the paper refers to the IRM report on the first Action Plan, including its main findings and the five recommendations in it – which reflect consultation by the IRM researcher and which have formal status in the OGP process. To understand the fundamental importance of the independent review process in the OGP, and the importance of all countries respecting it and protecting it, look at Samantha Power’s speech to the OGP Summit in Mexico City last October (Power was one of the architects of the OGP). To leave reference to the NZ IRM report out of the section on ‘what we learned’ is presumably an oversight that can be quickly rectified.

iii) The discussion of the fact that NZ scores highly on a number of international indices needs to be balanced by including discussion of concerns outside government about the state of integrity, openness, accountability, and public participation in NZ. Amongst other documents, the 2013 TINZ NIS assessment should be cited, as it represented a large-scale civil society exercise, involving extensive research and civil society consultations and input, on topics that are at the heart of the OGP.  Some of those consulted on NZ’s OGP Action Plan have expressed support for it and the usefulness of its recommendations for developing NZ’s Action Plan e.g. the PSA said ‘the NIS assessment provides the greatest opportunity for new actions’.

What does open government mean to you in practice?

A systematic and pro-active approach to greater direct and meaningful public engagement in the development of public policies, their implementation, monitoring and review. NZ scores well on a number of indices of transparency, but even the most transparent disclosure without public engagement does not necessarily lead to more accountability or better policies or improved outcomes. The latest generation of transparency and accountability initiatives – of which OGP is just one - are stressing direct public engagement (in combination with expanded disclosures), both as a democratic right of citizens, and for instrumental reasons i.e. public participation improves the quality, effectiveness and perhaps, most importantly, the legitimacy of public policies – witness the widespread public outcries in western democracies about the perceived unfairness of public policies, and about perceived capture of the public policy process by insiders and elites. Failure to address these concerns risks reducing the ability of NZ governments to govern effectively by being able to continue to draw on high levels of willingness to obey the law, pay taxes, comply with regulations, model ethical behaviour in their communities, volunteer, and to willingly provide social services under contracts with government that do not reflect the full costs.

Open government also means:

•               Increased transparency in key areas of weakness and concern, such as regular independent environmental and social reporting, political party funding, and lobbying of public officials.

•               More effective oversight of the executive by Parliament.

What do you think should be the themes of the second National Action Plan for Open Government should be? e.g. Open data, engagement, open budgets?

First theme: completing unfinished business from the first Action Plan i.e. responding properly to the 2013 TINZ NIS assessment and its 60 or so recommendations. Considering the NIS recommendations was a commitment in the first Action Plan, and there has been no published attempt by the government to demonstrate that this has occurred. To have credibility, the second plan needs to pick this commitment up, specify it properly in terms of OGP SMART criteria, and deal with it properly.

Secondly, the five recommendations in the IRM report have specific status in the OGP, and need to be given sufficient visibility as inputs into the process of developing the second action plan.

Thirdly, other suggested themes - many of which overlap with the IRM report’s recommendations – are:

a.             Official information Act: systematic pro-active disclosure; extend coverage to Parliamentary bodies; increase efforts to train public servants on their duties under the Act.

b.             Public engagement: a government-wide initiative to develop a pro-active, systematic and consistent approach to increased direct and meaningful public engagement with all communities, and especially traditionally unheard voices, across the policy cycle.

c.             Public service integrity: strengthened whistle blower laws and implementation; reinstatement of regular SSC surveys of public service conduct and integrity; a commitment to reviewing and protecting the role of non-partisan and free and frank advice by public servants to elected officials.

d.             Increased public disclosure: technically independent state of the nation social and environmental reporting; publish an annual Citizens’ Guide to the Budget and introduce further initiatives to raise citizen budget literacy; extend transparency of public procurement; and publish Ministers’ diaries.
