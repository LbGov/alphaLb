Submitted on 08/08/2016 2:46pm

What sector do you work in?

Museum, including research library

How often do you interact with the National Library of New Zealand?

Frequently

Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030? 

Yes

Why or why not?

In our view the themes, when considered as an integrated package, articulate the most important facets of leadership and areas of activity required of a national library for the foreseeable future - in literacy and digital literacy, which are essential for citizenship; collecting and preserving our nation's memories as taonga as both preserving the past and providing the platform for future innovation and creativity; and providing whole-of-country leadership in knowledge systems which expose New Zealanders to new research and create efficiencies across multiple sectors.

Do the strategic directions fit with your view of the role of a national library?

Yes

Why or why not?

The National Library, as a central government funded agency has a national co-ordination role that no other organisation in New Zealand is mandated to pick up. It can provide leadership and efficiency in systems such as national digital preservation,  that are not possible on an individual institution or local government basis. The central co-ordination and partnership model proposed, together with these strategic directions, represent best spend of public money.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Significantly scaled up digitisation of NZ content

Why?

Material which is uniquely of and from New Zealand is essential for research, creativity and innovation. In our view scaled up digitisation of NZ content would need to allow for the possibility of unpublished materials being as important or potentially of greater value for digitisation than published materials. Additionally, we suggest that analysis of user need would be required to guide the scaled up digitisation, to ensure a benefit for investment rationale. The document is silent on these considerations.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

National contracts for licensing e-content and new systems

Why?

We strongly support progressing national contracts for licensing e-content and new systems, in order to ensure ready access to the world's knowledge for all sectors in the NZ community. We appreciate this would be difficult to pull together but we believe it would be entirely achievable. The example of PHARMAC provides the nation with a useful working precedent.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

Cross-sector commitment for positive outcomes through reading

Why?

Today's students are the researchers, innovators and creators of tomorrow.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

National reading strategy based on successful literacy programmes

Why?

A co-ordinated strategy aligning the agendas of agencies across multiple sectors would be challenging to achieve. That said, we would support this approach as a good spend of public money. Consideration could be given to models such as the reduction in the road toll achieved by initiatives across multiple agencies addressing different facets of the same challenge.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

A national documentary heritage framework & strategy for institutional collaboration

Why?

A co-ordinated, partnership approach would support sustainable preservation of the nation's documentary memory. It would make sure the essential expertise is shared and materials are collected and preserved without duplication of effort. This is particularly important to avoid a 'black hole' in the nation's memory caused by under-collecting or inadequate preservation of digital-born heritage.

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A sustainable, fit-for-purpose digital preservation approach to manage new digital content

Why?

In our view this is technically difficult and expensive, but is necessary. We do not support blanket preservation of New Zealand content and strongly support the inclusion of sustainable and fit-for-purpose considerations.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

We have two comments: Firstly, we fully support the 'Words as Taonga' focus area and understand this to encompass the particular things that the National Library of New Zealand has at its heart: New Zealand's creations, music, cultural works, stories, meanings, creative outputs, language, usage etc. However the 'Words as taonga' phrase does not quite capture these concepts and could perhaps be revisited. We support the principles of targeted rather than 'blanket', value for money and outcome measurement and suggest these dimensions be tied into the three strong statements of need on page 2 to make the strategic directions more compelling.

How does the National Library’s draft strategic directions fit with your organisation’s priorities?

Strong fit with the Museum's strategic priorities: http://www.aucklandmuseum.com/about-us/future-museum

In which areas are you or your organisation interested in partnering with or contributing to the National Library?

Auckland Museum is interested in exploring partnering with the National Library in multiple initiatives under both 'One knowledge network' and 'Words as taonga'.

How would you like to be involved as the strategic directions are progressed?

As a potential partner

Any other thoughts you'd like to share?

We are grateful for the opportunity to provide feedback; please feel free to contact us with any queries related to this submission.

What sector do you work in?

Local government

How often do you interact with the National Library of New Zealand?

Sometimes

Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030? 

Yes

Why or why not?

Partly. As broad themes these are fine in terms of areas of focus. I do feel the theme ‘a nation of readers’ has a sense of being dumbed down but realise we live in an age of sound bites. I think it would be more accurate to refer to literacy; whether people choose to read or not is not the issue, it is the capacity to read that matters and allows for choice. I note on page two you refer to improving literacy, but relate it solely to social participation and work skills, when research shows considerably broader benefits.

Do the strategic directions fit with your view of the role of a national library?

Yes

Why or why not?

The themes fit well with your role to “collect, protect and share” when read in conjunction with the Horizon 1, 2 and 3 opportunities and suggestions for what success could look like. Without that context, they have little substance but, again, I acknowledge the need for sound bites and elevator pitches.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Redeveloped digital services for better interaction with National Library collections

Why?

I believe New Zealanders should have simple access to the collections held by National Library because so much of this material is unique and has incredible socio-historic value for people. With easier access there is potential for curation and co-creation, and for these collections to be the spark that ignites new works. This should be your unique selling point because access to these collections is something only you can provide.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Local government and schools taking part in a NZ-wide shared library system

Why?

A minor concern with this is around managing users who move a lot and may leave a trail of fines and lost items in their wake. Of far more concern is the idea of one library management system (LMS) given the current problems with Kotui, and the annual cost of the service. I realise an LMS might simply be a discovery layer pulling together information from many systems, and that Kotui as a service might not mean the current SirsiDynix system. I could not justify the cost of the Kotui service when we can easily run and improve Koha for less than 10% of that cost; it would be a waste of ratepayer money. If you go down this route I would expect to see an open and transparent procurement process which included Koha, as a locally developed open source system which is used by major consortia overseas and has satisfied users within New Zealand. It is also a concern to me that there is continued grumbling about the SirsiDynix product and vendor response, yet Kotui the service seems reluctant to acknowledge this and deal with it.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

Distribute te reo material through e-devices to te reo Māori learners

Why?

I see the value in this, and would love to see the material available to learners who are not in formal learning institutions, but feel getting agreement on content, regional dialect variations etc will make this very hard to implement.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A sustainable, fit-for-purpose digital preservation approach to manage new digital content

Why?

This outcome is in Horizon 3 but I don’t believe it can wait that long if digital collections have increased tenfold in 5 years and continue to grow exponentially. Every year this work is delayed means more and more content is not adequately preserved, making the eventual work harder and the potential loss of digital documentary heritage greater. I think it is vital this work is begun as soon as practical so that institutions across New Zealand can work efficiently towards digital preservation using one set of well-understood, achievable standards.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

Shared expertise to acquire, curate & preserve our documentary heritage & make it accessible

Why?

The National Library Act refers to “The purpose of the National Library is to enrich the cultural and economic life of New Zealand and its interchanges with other nations by, as appropriate,— (a) collecting, preserving, and protecting documents, particularly those relating to New Zealand, and making them accessible for all the people of New Zealand, in a manner consistent with their status as documentary heritage and taonga.” This proposed outcome under Horizon 2 seems to begin moving responsibility to institutions outside of National Library. I am especially concerned about preservation, as I feel this is too specialised an area for most public libraries to be responsible for. If words as taonga is a main thread in the strategy, then the care of our documentary heritage must be the role of our specialist organisation, National Library. I believe we should expect you to be the ‘keepers of the last of the last’ particularly for New Zealand materials. I am concerned that smaller libraries, who have relied on National Library for ‘last of the last’, may lose this service but are not in a position to maintain large stacks themselves.

What sector do you work in?

Not for profit sector

How often do you interact with the National Library of New Zealand?

Sometimes

Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030? 

Yes

Why or why not?

Yes and No. 1.1. The idea of one knowledge network is transformative and its realization would lead to a more sustainable and accessible library system across New Zealand. It would be beneficial to print disabled New Zealanders, increasing access to a much wider range of content. 1.2. The emphasis on literacy is very broad, and could only be achieved through collaboration with a wide range of stakeholders, especially the education sector. This is transformative, from a passive provision of access to content, to taking on a literacy leadership role; but will require advanced relationship management, and a new skill set for staff. If the strategy included an inclusiveness component, it would suggest benefits for blind and low vision students. 1.3. There are aspects of the third key area that seem to blur the lines between institutions, and collaboration again will be key to success. There are components which seem like appropriate areas for National Library to lead. However, this seems like the least transformative key idea, containing more aspects that are business as usual and there is scope to be more aspirational.

Do the strategic directions fit with your view of the role of a national library?

Yes

Why or why not?

Yes and No. The proposed changes will help transform National Library from an institution into the leader in the New Zealand Library sector. This is vital, as there is a current lack of coherence, and leadership could itself be spelled out more strongly in the strategy. The call for collaboration, sharing, and reforming existing knowledge networks all make sense. It is meaningful to want to lead the literacy movement; a National Library is where we should be able to easily locate and celebrate important collections. However: 2.1. Can National Library lead a literacy reformation without a clear mandate from the education sector?

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Network connection linking digital content across all sectors

Why?

For the Blind Foundation, the concept of one knowledge network is the most important. Improving digital access to information to New Zealanders across the country aligns with our strategic direction. We aspire for all print disabled New Zealanders to have equitable information access, and this idea would help realize that, ensuring that geographic remoteness, licensing or local body funding models were not an impediment to access. The focus being placed on digital is relevant to the Blind Foundation, as one of our strategic goals is to have a 100% digital content library service by 2020. An outcome that we would like to help achieve is a National Print Disability Collection that is digital, download based, with curation, support and systems shared across the library sector. This would help ensure that the one knowledge network is inclusive and reaching all New Zealanders.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Local government and schools taking part in a NZ-wide shared library system

Why?

Only because this idea limits the access of other sector libraries to the network. Essentially, collaboration will be more beneficial to more New Zealanders if a broader range of libraries were included.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

National reading strategy based on successful literacy programmes

Why?

If achievable, there would be widespread benefit for New Zealand. In the context of our organization, a commitment to the literacy of print disabled youth would be beneficial, and we would be open to discussion about collaboration in this area.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

National reading strategy based on successful literacy programmes

Why?

This is such a paradigm shift, and so reliant upon partnerships outside the library sector, that it is difficult to picture success.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

A sustainable, fit-for-purpose digital preservation approach to manage new digital content

Why?

Digital access to more information has the potential to benefit print disabled New Zealanders who are unable to access traditional printed material. This would be dependent upon the digitization resulting in content being created in an accessible format. But the concept itself, of a wide ranging national digital collection, is appealing.

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

N/A

Why?

Key idea three lacks (within the consultation document) the sense of transformation that the other two ideas have. Is there scope to restate it, to be more aspirational with it, remembering that it is taking us to 2030? To have ongoing relevance at that point, and to be something other than what it is now, National Library will need to be the centre for Maori and Pacific content; the centre for cultural discourse; a world-leading digital repository; the clear voice of library sector leadership.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

There is no mention of equitable access, or inclusiveness. Cultural diversity does capture the changing demographic within New Zealand, but it is difficult to find a commitment to inclusiveness within the document, and there is scope to strengthen that. Equitable access is a term used globally, including within key United Nations information frameworks, and it would be powerful to include in this document. It is a term that signifies the removal of barriers, and that the work will be done to support all New Zealanders forwards into an inclusive information society.

How does the National Library’s draft strategic directions fit with your organisation’s priorities?

The strategic directions in the draft document align closely with key objectives within the Blind Foundation’s strategic plan 2015-2020. These include an objective to contribute to the availability of a print disability collection in every public library by 2020, which aligns with the concept of a one knowledge network. Our library system is also targeting 100% digital content delivery by 2020. As an organization, we are also looking to partner with national and community organizations that can help us support vision impaired New Zealanders in their local community. The goal is for our clients to be active participants in their local communities. In this context, with a single knowledge network, local public libraries would become a primary destination for print disabled New Zealanders seeking information, or support in accessing information. Our youth library system is also seeking to broaden the access our younger clients have to information. The nation of readers’ idea supports this drive. Our primary goal for these students is to ensure they have authentic literacy skills, a key building block for a productive adult and working life.

In which areas are you or your organisation interested in partnering with or contributing to the National Library?

The Blind Foundation would be interested in partnering with National Library to develop a National Print Disability Collection. This collection would be digital; accessible to all New Zealanders with a print disability; have registration and access points at all New Zealand public libraries; curation would be led by the Blind Foundation, who have a specialism in accessible formats; be distributed through the already-developed BookLink app; have a collaborative governance group consisting of National Library, public library and Blind Foundation staff. The desire for partnership is because the Blind Foundation cannot drive this unsupported; and because it will benefit New Zealanders on a much wider scale than those who are vision impaired. We would look to National Library to help drive the concept and implementation.

How would you like to be involved as the strategic directions are progressed?

As a potential partner

Any other thoughts you'd like to share?

The Blind Foundation would like to collaborate with National Library in helping to create an accessible format digital collection that helps to address the existing book famine for print disabled New Zealanders. With the expected ratification of the Marrakesh Treaty, a much larger amount of accessible format content is going to become available to print disabled New Zealanders, and we see a shared role across the library sector to work to create a coherent, national system that enables more equitable access to information. We feel that we have a key role to play as a collaborator in ensuring that the information needs of this growing community within NZ are met in the context of one knowledge network.

Submitted on 21/07/2016 1:03pm

What sector do you work in?

general "informed citizen" interest, retired person with career in library / museum / publishing sector incl. various roles at NLNZ.

How often do you interact with the National Library of New Zealand?

Frequently

Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030? 

Yes

Why or why not?

Consistent with NL purpose and with 2016 realities of demographic trends, technology literacy, public-good provision as distinct from competitive marketplace provision

Do the strategic directions fit with your view of the role of a national library?

Yes

Why or why not?

Yes up to a point. There is risk of over-emphasis about NZ-produced material which is a tiny fragment of total knowledge and of relatively minor impact on NZ's social and economic prosperity. The real need is for NZers to be able to learn and have access from the outside world, which is part of the NL statutory purpose but is severely understated in this plan.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Significantly scaled up digitisation of NZ content

Why?

Build on the proven success of "Papers Past". All the other "proposed ideas for success" seem wishful thinking and risk wasted effort akin to the NDIS of the 1990s. There are plenty of market solutions which the NL can encourage and participate in, not necessarily through the "mainstream public library" network which is largely "recreational" in actual use and perception.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

National contracts for licensing e-content and new systems

Why?

The equivalent of offering discounted print book supply. Hard to see the actual purpose and benefit being sought. Ordinary competitive market forces will build progress if this is an actual area of need. Looks like a solution looking for a problem to solve.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

A cross-sector partnership to improve literacy levels

Why?

If literacy really is as big a problem for NZ as it is made out to be, then this must be the NL area of focus. "Cross-sector partnerships" will have to look beyond the comfortable "mainstream library" sector and NL will have to be prepared to work in different ways with different on-the-ground agencies.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Public library membership from birth & early childhood 

Why?

If it is that important at the city level then the PLs should be doing it already. Hard to see what added value an input by NLNZ would make.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

A sustainable, fit-for-purpose digital preservation approach to manage new digital content

Why?

Emphasis on sustainable i.e. able to be nimble and entrepreneurial and actively aware of developments and trends in social media and in "publishing" generally.

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A national documentary heritage framework & strategy for institutional collaboration

Why?

Again, if it is that important it should surely have been achieved already. There is an element of "herding cats" here which the NL would be unwise to get bogged down in. The real world moves on.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

1. The NL role in ensuring that all people across NZ have good access to the ideas and knowledge of the wider external world is very understated here. NZ's success surely depends on this more than on an inward-looking focus of nurturing home-grown knowledge , important though the latter may be. The access to the world's knowledge has been part of the NL's public good purpose since the 1930s and the NL should be assured that the outcomes are in fact being sustainably met before abandoning this purpose. 2. Effective alternative local delivery channels. There is an emphasis on the public library network, but in reality this is mainly a recreational / leisure type role as exemplified in their governance arrangements and financing. It should be presumed that this NZ PL sector will undergo the same sort of wind-back as now happening in the UK; the NL would be unwise to assume that it is an effective local channel for NL delivery. So put more emphasis on direct-access channels as are used e.g. for accessing "Papers past" etc.

NL should be spending more time understanding the publishing industry and its trends. The NL offers direct access to citizens via (i) off-the-street access to its Wellington "National Cultural Institution" site and (ii) direct on-line access as for "Papers Past" etc. The plan would be better positioned if it emphasised this direct access capability and made its customer interface as intuitive and easy-to-use as other direct access resources which ordinary internet users are well accustomed to use.
