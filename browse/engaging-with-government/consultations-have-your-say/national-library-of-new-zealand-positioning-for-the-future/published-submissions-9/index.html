Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Local government & schools taking part in a shared library system

Why?

There is real potential to create a more joined up and seemless expereince for our users across the state. One area that the National Library might focus is the Community Archive. The Community Archive is an important catalogue, electronic database and website supporting community archives groups and historical associations of all sizes. For these communities it is the knowledge network that allows them to showcase their collections to the research community online, hosted by Archives NZ. Due to limited resources it is often the only outlet many communities have to connect with their users/researchers and communities online. Currently the Community Archive is underfunded and undersupported, but has real potential to be a central repository for collections and organisations of all size. Collaborative leadership on the part of the National Library to support, grow, and further develop the Community archive as the central place for cultural heritage institutions of all shapes and sizes to share descriptive access to collections would go far toward creating One Knowledge Network for heritage collections. Especially if this service could be linked with Digital NZ, enabling access to descriptive records and digital records where available.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Significantly scaled up digitisation of NZ content

Why?

I agree that increased digitisation brings increased access to our collections, but I worry about the environmental impacts of mass digitisation. With the impacts of climate change already clearly visible for our island nation, as well as our Pacific neighbours, I would like the environmental impacts of mass digitisation taken into accout. What would be the costs, both monitarily and environmentally, in storing, for perpituity, all those digital images in vast server farms?

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

Programmes to inspire reading & support literacy

Why?

I would like to see the library take a more active role in inspiring reading and writing through public programs such as author talks, residencies, and related events. I think the other ideas are very good as well, but will take a real commitment to working collaborativly across sectors. Expanding literacy to digital literacy would also be nice as those with low literacy generally are also more likely to be locked out or experience digital divide issues.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Distribute te reo material through e-devices to te reo Māori learners

Why?

how about just distribute te reo material without dealing with the e-devices. Keep the technology out of it and there is a much better change for sucess.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

Shared expertise to acquire, curate & preserve our documentary heritage & make it accessible

Why?

I like almost all the ideas, but I do think there is a real oportunity for the National Library to be a leader through training and shared expertise. We have some of the most highly trained and skilled practioners in the country and we should be trying to share our skills and increase the skills and expertise of librarians and others across the GLAM sector

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A sustainable, fit-for-purpose digital preservation approach to manage new digital content

Why?

This needs to happen, but will require a real will to invest in infrastructure and people. There has been a lot of talk about our digital preservation programme in the last few years, but no real investment in continual improvement. It feels like we are treading water or falling behind while we're watching others around the world doing innovative things we can't do.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

Any specific ideas around born digital collecting (as opposed to digitisation or digital preservation). We do not currently have strategies, tools, expertise, mandate or vision to collect today’s digital heritage. We're currently unable and unresourced in either tools or people to collect born digital heritage collections. A strategy that recognised the importance of collecting NZ changing demographics will need to incorporate new ways of digital collecting.

How does the National Library’s draft strategic directions fit with your organisation’s priorities?

The Lesbian and Gay Archives of NZ (LAGANZ) thanks the National Library for the opportunity to comment on key strategic directions for future consideration. LAGANZ is New Zealand’s primary archive of memory for the Lesbian, Gay, Trans, Bisexual, Intersex, Takatāpui, Fa'afafine and all its related communities (LGBTITF) in New Zealand and it enjoys a very special and unique relationship with the Alexander Turnbull Library who are responsible for the custody and care of collections held on LAGANZ behalf.

In which areas are you or your organisation interested in partnering with or contributing to the National Library?

The Lesbian and Gay Archives of New Zealand (LAGANZ) Trust was formed in 1992. It’s Board of Trustees own, on behalf of the lesbian and gay communities, the growing number of collections which now make up the Archives. The initial core of the collections goes back to an initiative of the National Gay Rights Coalition in 1977. It developed into a research library and archives known as the Lesbian and Gay Rights Resource Centre which was placed in the care of a trust in 1984. In 1988, to ensure the collections a secure home, the trustees made an agreement with the Alexander Turnbull Library in Wellington for their permanent accommodation. Collections are actively being grown to capture the diversity and growth of LGBTITF communities in New Zealand. The collections remain under the ownership of the Trust, and are open to researchers on the same basis as other research heritage collections. This agreement secures their safe storage and assures that access to them is supervised.

Keep me informed

Any other thoughts you'd like to share?

From a LAGANZ perspective: One knowledge network is a tāonga - visibility and accessibility to LGBTITF material / collections wherever they might be across institutions in NZ including libraries is important for community. Feeling LGBTI knowledge is valued and respected is also very important for the same community, so the values and principles that underpin one knowledge network are equally important. The relationship between LAGANZ and the Alexandra Turnbull Library is underpinned by mutual respect for what each other seeks to achieve. A nation of readers - reading opens the door wider for access to the history and aspirations of LGBTI communities, organisations and individuals. Words as a tāonga - words are tāonga that can never be taken back. Once words are said or written they can be open to scrutiny and critique. Importantly they reflect a snapshot of a person, community or organisation's thoughts or processes at a particular point or period in time and help to build a picture for both current and future generations; thus their transformation into tāonga or treasures. Words as tāonga reflects the commitment of LAGANZ to provide an appropriate environment to hold the memories of LGBTITF individuals and communities.

Submitted on 31/08/2016 3:56pm

What sector do you work in?

School

How often do you interact with the National Library of New Zealand?

Sometimes

Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030? 

No

Why or why not?

The broad themes of "information access for New Zealanders" and "collecting and preserving documents" are good and relate to the purpose of NLNZ in the legislation. However, I feel that the third theme "a nation of readers" is not really the core business of NLNZ. Improving literacy in NZ (the ability to read) is really the role of the Ministry of Education and encouraging New Zealanders to improve their literacy by providing a range of resources they will want to read is one of the roles of school and public libraries. NZNL could back them up in this through interloans but it should not be one of three major strategic directions. If a third strategic direction is desired, supporting, encouraging and promoting high quality New Zealand writing would be more appropriate as would the provision of high quality, affordable opportunities for librarians to upskill and ensure that libraries in New Zealand are well resourced with competent, up-to-date, professional staff. (Who will then be well equipped to reach out to their communities and encourage reading, social participation and finding information.)

Do the strategic directions fit with your view of the role of a national library?

No

Why or why not?

I see the role of a national library as safeguarding a nation's literary heritage and providing access to it for the people in that nation. Additionally, it may encourage the creation of this literature and information. It is also a 'backstop' for libraries in the nation, especially public, tertiary, school and not-for-profit libraries. A library for libraries in the way the Reserve Bank is a bank for banks. It should support researchers, although not necessarily research purely for commercial gain. This document appears almost to suggest that the purpose of NLNZ will be to encourage as much financial gain as possible with the smallest amount of investment it can get away with. New Zealand has other agencies for that.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Significantly scaled up digitisation of NZ content

Why?

I have found it increasingly difficult to access New Zealand content. It has become harder to borrow books on interloan and most of what I am needing is not available digitally.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Local government and schools taking part in a NZ-wide shared library system

Why?

Partly because of the way they are resourced. School libraries (in state schools) are funded by central government through school operations grants. They resource the curriculum schools are required to deliver. Local government libraries are funded by ratepayers. They want their rates to buy resources of general interest to their community, not to resource school curriculum at the expense of their information and reading preferences. They may not be able to afford the staffing, space or information resources to do both. The second reason is that I would be a bit concerned about the integrity of subject headings. Most schools teach, and prefer to use, the simpler subject heading designed for school catalogues rather than the LoC system which is much more complex and too detailed for the needs of school libraries. Many students find the search skills hard enough to use and grasp on the simpler system but it is a really good place to begin teaching electronic search skills they can later further develop on electronic databases and beyond. This could be lost if our resources were catalogued on a system shared beyond our sector.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

NZ Children’s Laureate appointed to champion reading

Why?

Because I have to pick one from a drop-down list. They all seem to be beyond the scope of the NLNZ really. I'm not sure the best academic or reader in NZ is the person to encourage children to read more (because s/he will be too busy reading) but it's also hard to see how the captain of the All Blacks (who may have the pulling power) can spend too much time modelling and promoting reading.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A cross-sector partnership to improve literacy levels

Why?

Because I have to pick one. They are all problematic. It seems vague, idealistic, unworkable and would likely add another expensive layer of bureaucracy.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

A sustainable, fit-for-purpose digital preservation approach to manage new digital content

Why?

I think preserving digital content could be problematic as technology continues to develop and content grows exponentially.

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A state-of-the-art National Library space for discussion & debate

Why?

Many New Zealanders would not be able to use it because of distance. Robust discussion and debate can happen anywhere and does not rely on a 'state-of-the-art' space. It is the content that matters more than the setting.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

The feeling that NLNZ is a trustworthy guardian of our national literary soul and the documents we treasure as a nation. The reassurance that NLNZ is behind what we do in our libraries to encourage reading and link people with the information they want. The certainty of every person in New Zealand having access to information regardless of their geographic location, technical skill, areas of interest or bank balance.

How does the National Library’s draft strategic directions fit with your organisation’s priorities?

Not very well. We would like to feel that NZNL could better support school libraries with resources we are not able to afford or store ourselves but may need from time to time and with relevant, affordable face-to-face professional development so we can keep up with the changing information landscape and do our jobs well.

In which areas are you or your organisation interested in partnering with or contributing to the National Library?

We would like to work with NZNL to ensure resources for the primary and secondary state education sector are relevant, well-pitched, up-to-date and well maintained.

How would you like to be involved as the strategic directions are progressed?

As a potential partner

Any other thoughts you'd like to share?

I am concerned that if schools become "more connected to the collective resources of public libraries", regional inequality could increase. I am unhappy that we were told last year that school libraries could turn to the internet rather than relying so heavily on non-fiction books and are now told that a mere 14% of New Zealand published works are available online and they are subject to pay wall and broken links. Extending public library membership to everyone from birth does not mean that everyone will be willing or able to make use of that (eg distance, device access, print disability, inability to use the public library system, disinterest). As far as I know, membership is free for ratepayers and local residents at all public libraries but 'over 2 million' members would indicate that half (or more) of the NZ population has chosen not to take advantage of this or is unaware they can. There is neither the flexibility nor the word-space to say everything I would like to in this feedback format.

How often do you interact with the National Library of New Zealand?

Frequently

Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030? 

Yes

Why or why not?

generally agree that all three focus areas will shift the library sector to be better positioned for the future, however the National Library's role in that will alter depending on the initative undertaken. What could be made more explicit is how the three themes will be interconnected towards achieving the aspiration of a proporous New Zealand. Looking ahead to a horizon of 2030, with technological advances, urbanisation, increased migration, and other societal and financial pressures - the role of a library is likely to evolve. The ability for the final focus areas to flex in this rapidly changing and dynamic world will be important, and in my opinion, the need for documentary heritage, digital literacy and citizenship, improved connectedness, increased cababilty to access resource and information, and the nation's ability to read, are all strong foundations for positioning the library sector for the future. Libraries, including the National Library, need to adapt to this fast-paced changing environment by transitioning from traditional models, whilst understanding the role our libraries currently play in our communities is critical. Challenging status quo can be difficult and controversial, but I admire the outward-focussed ambition the discussion document presents with some of the opportunities identified.

Do the strategic directions fit with your view of the role of a national library?

Yes

Why or why not?

Under the approach of collaboration and working together, I feel there is a good fit between the potential role of a 'national library of the future' to achieve the aspirations outlined in the document. Success will require more than just a national library however, many partners will be needed to realise these aspirations. Relationships, partnerships and collaboration are all key to success - however are also very resource heavy / time consuming. Ensuring a national library is structured with appropriate capacity and skillset will therefore be important so that the library is agile enough to be proactive, can move with pace, and has the ability to be creative.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Significantly scaled up digitisation of NZ content

Why?

Ensuring NZ's history is documented and this knowledge is shared for future generations is important, and in my view digitisation, while problematic and expensive, is crucial to ensure the longevity and accessiblity of key resources. Further to this the impact that greater sharing of knowledge can have on education, economic growth and what it means to be a New Zealander can also be significant. Crucial to the success of this would be a stronger focus on digitial literacy and citizenship which doesn't appear to have come through as a strong theme in the dicussion docuement.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Local government and schools taking part in a NZ-wide shared library system

Why?

While I do not totally disagree with the concept or its ambition, in response to this question I do feel this would be problematic to implement across the library sector. To be successful it would need to be an opt-in service (even if at first) to ensure its impact reaches the targeted audience, and it would need alignment to a shared vision for a shared library network and capability development for the sector. It's not unachieveble, but a question to consider is whether the benefits of economies of scale, greater co-ordination and improved access through one library network outweighs the costs that would be associated with this project.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

National reading strategy based on successful literacy programmes

Why?

A national reading strategy could not only lead to a nation of readers, but also a nation of thinkers and a nation of inspired curiosity. What makes this theme so important is that the other two themes are reliant on a population being able to read for them to also be successful. A reading strategy with a distinct collaborative focus ensuring our population 'can' and 'do' read is important. The project also has the potential to be the cornerstone for the Nation of Readers theme to ensure an ongoing creative and innovative dialogue around other potential projects and partnerships post-adoption of a strategic document.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Public library membership from birth & early childhood

Why?

Similar to my comments around the LMS project, ensuring the benefits outweigh the costs of a project of this nature would need assessment. As it stands, while I understand what it this project is trying to achieve, I'm not convinced this is the answer to creating a nation of readers. Alternatively a 'national readers week' initiative that was part of a co-ordinated national reading strategy could be a huge driver for people of all ages (not just childhood) to have a library card and more successfully meet the objectives of this initative. While incredibly complex, there could be something around a universal library membership which may also be worth exploring.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

Digitisation and distribution of te reo Māori published content

Why?

Maori (including te reo) and Pacific collections and content are hugely important to our nation.

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A state-of-the-art National Library space for discussion & debate

Why?

Making it possible for the population outside of Wellington to see and engage, discuss and debate NZ's documentary hertitage in a virtual space will be important in the future. The emphasis of physical space in my opinion could be less important for the National Library over time, and the role of a 'library' compared to a museum or community hub is something that may become more grey and problemtic in defining 'what is a library' into the future.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

- Things in 2030 will be very different to 2016. Despite a small amount of context this document is largely silent on the potential challenges the library sector could face in the next 15 years, and therefore its unclear on the 'state of the future' that the document is trying to position itself for - Second to that, a stronger connection needs to be made between the interconnected nature between the three theme areas and the positioning that is being proposed for this potential future state - digital literacy and citizenship require greater visability and proactive planning. The library sector has a role in this - there may be work across the sector in preparing the library workforce for the future. Its likely going to require different skillsets than the library workforce of 2016 - and with that a project with tertiray institutions focussed around career development pathways could be important to look at.
