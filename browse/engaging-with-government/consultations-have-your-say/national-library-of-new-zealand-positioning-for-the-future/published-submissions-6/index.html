What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

There is opportunity to collaborate more closely with the global community of libraries and service providers. In many ways, this global collaboration is embodied in the new open source project, FOLIO, that has brought libraries and commercial entities together to re-envision library technology. FOLIO can support the objectives of the ‘one knowledge network’ by connecting libraries and users in innovative ways, supporting not just access to content, but driving towards new services. The community at large - libraries and vendors - can work together to imagine and develop these services and deliver applications such as predictive analytics or machine learning. Moreover, the project can drive student reading engagement and information literacy of children and adults through creative means such as gamification or social networks. Library membership can be promoted through ‘loyalty’ programs that build on a user’s interests and encourage engagement with the library. And the project can support the description of unique digital collections and support discovery and access from within different environments such as learning and content management systems. As such, the project aligns well with the goals outlined by the National Library of New Zealand and its quest to position itself for the future.

Any other thoughts you'd like to share?

Technology in libraries has largely remained stagnant: library management platforms often remain closed, are built on legacy architectures and are monolithic in nature. By contrast, many other industries have seen their ecosystems open up and deliver unprecedented innovation. These industries have witnessed a growth in collaborative and innovative projects and patents are made available. Companies like Google and Facebook make a variety of open source technologies available to spur innovation and help bring about radical change by harnessing the power of global participation, knowledge and talent. The time has come for the library industry to follow suit. The FOLIO project, a community collaboration to develop an open source platform, will support traditional library management functionality, yet is built for innovation. The FOLIO platform will be extensible at its core, so anyone can freely build on core functionality or extend the platform through the development of applications that will deliver new services. Libraries can leverage the platform’s core functionality, utilize existing applications, and either develop new applications independently or work with other libraries, any developer or commercial vendor. The project, which is well underway, is bound to deliver new approaches to the challenges that libraries face in an ever-changing world.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Local government & schools taking part in a shared library system

Why?

Schools and public libraries are typically under funded with resource deficiencies - doing more with less. A shared library management system will: • Provide libraries infrastructure where they can realise cost efficiencies both potentially in the short and longer term • Provide a platform for libraries to leverage regional expertise • Encourage libraries to work together in a collaborative way • Encourage cross sector collaboration • Create workflows and efficiencies • Sharing of bibliographic data

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

National contracts for licensing e-content and new systems

Why?

Negotiating national contracts for electronic content will be potentially easier than negotiating national contracts for systems -due to the level of system complexity required, differing levels of expertise amongst libraries, and current systems in place. There may be challenges in negotiation these national contracts when the starting line is so disparate. However, content negotiation would be far easier, and the success of the EPIC consortia is testament to this.

Why?

Fostering a love of reading from an early age is fundamental to the enjoyment in to childhood, teenage years and beyond. Providing public library membership from birth is a great way to engage and foster this love. It also provides a safe haven for children and a sense of continuity and well being in their life.

No

Why or why not?

well yes and no. They are appropriate to the role of a national library, However given that this is already 2016, and the purpose is positioning for the future, I don't find the initiatives to be particularly bold or ambitious. Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030? 

No

Why or why not?

Partially. The biggest challenge for citizens, businesses, organisations, including libraries, is the ubiquitous nature of digital and the rapid pace of technological change. The next 17 years until 2030 is unlikely to be any different. Therefore the National Library's strategic directions need to focus on how it fulfills its purposes, as outlined in the Act and which are equally important, so that New Zealanders can benefit from the opportunities of our hyper-connected, digital world. The first theme "One Knowledge network" addresses some of these challenges as does "Words as Taonga". However, the Nation of Readers, while important does not address the significant issues of digital literacy, data literacy and the need for people to have a much broader range of skills to effectively participate in a digital world. It seems to me that the focus area is more likely to be part of a bigger theme around creativity, innovation and productivity of which literacy and reading is a subset.

Do the strategic directions fit with your view of the role of a national library?

Yes

Why or why not?

A partial yes. The National Library's role is defined in the Act. The report on the consultation with stakeholders clearly supported the key role that NL plays in "ensuring current and future generations have access to recorded knowledge and services that enable them to live, work and innovate in this digital, global world. They need: • Content and collections that are freely available and easy to discover, use and reuse; available over time for future generations; • Infrastructure, both physical and digital, that supports the exchange of knowledge and data both between systems and tools and between people; • The skills and expert support to make use of the knowledge they need to be productive and engaged citizens. National Library fulfills this mission both directly through its collections and services to individual researchers and other users, and indirectly through its support of other libraries and schools and its collaboration with similar cultural and collecting institutions. This network of libraries, museums, galleries and archives collectively provides public access to the knowledge and stories important to our nation, linked by a common purpose of enabling greater understanding, social cohesion, cultural diversity, learning and the creation of new knowledge and innovations."

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Significantly scaled up digitisation of NZ content

Why?

Mass digitisation of NZ content is long overdue. NL has been successful with Papers Past and providing easy access to the digital collections held in other institutions from the GLAM sector and others through Digital NZ. Digitisation of NZ published items needs to happen on a mass scale - dont worry about whether someone else has already digitised it overseas - a lot of time and money could be spent trying to work out what has been done and what hasn't which could be spent on the mass digitisation. This is NZ knowledge and should be under NZ care and preservation. If there are two digitised copies in the world this may be a good thing from the point of access and preservation.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Partnership with rights management organisations to publish NZ’s output digitally

Why?

Getting more NZ content in a digital format has two facets - one is the digitisation of existing print material and the other the acquisition of born digital material (or published both in print and digital). Out of copyright and should be done now. The material still within copyright is more problematic and is probably an horizon 2 or 3 area. Making electronic legal deposit work in a much less manual way and ensuring that ePublished materials are routinely collected and in the NDHA will require a climate of trust with publishers and authors. Building this relationship, being clearly able to articulate not only the why but the how this will happen and the safeguards for publishers and authors is not going to be easy. But it does need to be tacked. A review of the Public Lending Right for a digital world is also vital. And the fund should be increased as it has not been for a significant period of time. This is a tangible way that government can met competing objectives for access to knowledge with ensuring appropriate recompense for the product of the creative process.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

A cross-sector partnership to improve literacy levels

Why?

I the National Library wishes to continue with this strategic priority as it stands then the most important area for action is in taking a lead on trying to bring the various agencies, including libraries to take a collective impact approach to literacy. I see this as a five year research project where all the different agencies sign up to an agreed set of measures and a concerted program of activity, seeded by government. Alongside the programme of activity and measurement one of the Universities could put a research framework around this to truly determine what the longer term benefits of this focus might be.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Public library membership from birth & early childhood

Why?

I think a fabulous goal to have is "every child a reader" A major way of achieving this might be to have every child a member of a public library from birth. Achieving this should be easy but I suspect it will be quite problematic!

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

A sustainable, fit-for-purpose digital preservation approach to manage new digital content

Why?

NL has already invested in the development of the NDHA and we need to build on this to ensure the ongoing improvement of systems - particularly those that provide simple and fast access, and can deal with web harvest on a regular basis. This will need investment and it is an area ripe for collaboration both internationally and at home. Some universities (Victoria University Library for one) have purchased Rosetta and National Library has expertise that can be shared but also can learn from what others are doing. This area can not be seen as a money making service provided by National Library to other institutions but a collaborative approach where partners pool resources, both financial and intellectual to get the best for the country. It would be good to know what happened to the business case for National Preservation as Service.

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A national documentary heritage framework & strategy for institutional collaboration

Why?

There have been two attempts to do this in the past and mostly they have not landed anywhere, largely I suspect because if it is acknowledged that we have a distributed heritage collection model then government is worried that other institutions will seek funding from the central coffers. However the reality is that our documentary heritage IS distributed amongst different cultural, tertiary and public institutions. ATL may be a preeminent collection but it doesn't collect everything either. Where do we find our scientific documentary heritage? Who holds unique materials? The issue it seems to me is we need to understand the drivers for why we want a national documentary heritage framework. Is it because we want to get more funding, or more equitable funding? Is it because we want the public to be aware of the key institutions, a consciousness raising with stakeholders? It it because we want flush out little know collections and make the public aware of what is in those collections? What is the place of non New Zealand and Pacific materials in such a framework? Once we know what is wanted from such a framework we might have better success.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

1. the National Library's role (through ATL) of supporting scholarly research including the independent scholar - a more proactive role in demonstrating that the humanities, social sciences and history have much to teach us about our country, its place in the world. Digital humanities is where NL could interface with the tertiary sector, particularly University libraries. A more proactive approach in fostering innovation and creativity - social entrepreneurship - through ATL. 2. the strategic importance of the services that National Library provides in collaboration with other libraries or on behalf of other libraries. It is too hidden and not properly resourced. The role that public libraries play in making real One knowledge network was dramatically advanced when the APNK was initiated nearly ten years ago. It is urgent that a refreshed model happens now and does not wait until there is more flesh on the bones of the strategy. There should be no question that this is a legitimate part of the NL's purpose and as such it should continue to be partially funded by government. 3. Further down the track the NL needs to have a clear funding strategy for the delivery of its purposes under the Act.

It is great to see the NL looking towards its future and how it best contributes to "turning knowledge into value". The language of the document will be important. Others I note have commented on 'reading' and 'words' as being limiting and not really getting to grips with the wider issues and I agree. The plan claims to be bold - and I encourage you and the government to be bolder. This will need investment - often quite modest investment will make a huge difference - the $2m added to the NL baseline in 2006/7 made a huge difference to how NZers accessed content through the birth of Digital NZ and APNK. This kind of investment is modest in government terms. Mass digitisation is a bigger investment but the longer term benefits for getting NZ content to where the eyes are and making it available for research and reuse will be significant.
