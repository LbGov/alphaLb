One knowledge network is required to make the library network sustainable and effective in the future. A nation of readers and Words as Taonga focuses on core library outputs, and a clear focus on people and the content. I don't like the actual theme titles, but the summaries make sense.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Redeveloped digital services for better interaction with National Library collections

Why?

Services need to be digital first, otherwise they are essentially invisible now. This digital first approach could then more easily enable other networks to connect. However, services also need content, so digitisation and content partnerships are vital as well.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Partnership with rights management organisations to publish NZ’s output digitally

Why?

There is still a clash of ideals in various sectors and areas of government that will make it difficult, as it always has been, to find an appropriate balance of public good/social growth, and creator and publisher revenue.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

A cross-sector partnership to improve literacy levels

Why?

The library itself probably can't/won't have enough leverage to have as big an impact as it could if it partnered. Together the partners could reach areas of society that could only be reached through a connected network. New Zealand is too small to NOT work together. The various orgs have different strengths and weaknesses, some overlaps in community, and no real reason NOT to work together. Much more could be achieved through a co-ordinated and shared effort. With a rapidly changing cultural face of the nation, one organisation is not going to be able to keep up with all the communities needs.

NZ Children’s Laureate appointed to champion reading

Why?

I'm not sure it would have a significant impact.

The rate of growth of Auckland, the growing diversity of the population and the ever-changing expectation of physical and digital citizens, and the digital and economic divides mean the library alone won't alone be able to reach the people it needs to best collect, care for and share our documentary heritage. NZ is too small for the main organisations, and as well as smaller community based groups to be duplicating effort and cost. Shared networks and strategies would better serve our small but diverse country.

Many people who are engaged or affected by this debate do not even know the library is a forum, let alone one they would turn to first.

Submitted on 31/08/2016 4:32pm

What sector do you work in?

Gallery/museum

How often do you interact with the National Library of New Zealand?

Frequently

Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030? 

Yes

Why or why not?

These summarise a broad range of issues which a national library can and should address.

Do the strategic directions fit with your view of the role of a national library?

Yes

Why or why not?

While there may be other themes or ways of stating them, these acknowledge the current context and future possibilities here in Aotearoa New Zealand.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Significantly scaled up digitisation of NZ content

Why?

Digitization is an area where NLNZ has a proven track record, capacity to scale up, and can take a leadership role across the GLAM sectors.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Partnership with rights management organisations to publish NZ’s output digitally

Why?

This is a great idea. The possible stumbling block is our Copyright Act, and also where our legislation sits internationally - these will need to be reviewed before any real progress can be achieved.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

A cross-sector partnership to improve literacy levels

Why?

Cross-sector partnerships are essential to cut across the barriers of siloed resourcing, eg the vital and intersecting issues of literacy and access to social services, education, health, employment... This also applies to the idea of 'A strong cross-sector commitment to achieve sustained social, cultural and economic outcomes for communities through the promotion of reading'.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

N/A

Why?

N/A

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

A national documentary heritage framework &amp; strategy for institutional collaboration

Why?

Documentary heritage (and new written/documentary/digital material of all kinds) is collected and held in a wide range of public (and private) institutions. NLNZ is in the best position to lead on a collaborative framework for preservation and access - setting standards and improving capability nationally. Working with Archives NZ under the DIA umbrella, and more broadly with other leading GLAMS organisations, a strategy which reaches out to all our citizens would assist and empower smaller organisations, and could extend to the Pacific and private collectors as well

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

New National Library programmes encouraging learning about constitutional documents

Why?

The ideas of public programmes, space for discussion and debate, and strengthened collections need to be progressed in partnership with other collecting and exhibiting organisations across the GLAM sectors. We already have a 'distributed national collection' held in a wide range of institutions, and a blurring of the roles of galleries, libraries, archives and museums with respect to public engagement. It is essential that our limited resources are used to best effect across all these so our shared goals are respected, our citizens and communities benefit from the differences in approach and emphasis, and we avoid gaps in provision.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

This draft strategy outlines some really important goals and ways to work towards them. The only thing I would add is that our increasingly digital world makes us increasingly connected internationally as well as nationally. It also widens the gaps between those that are and are not connected - and there are many within Aotearoa New Zealand who are not. Online, it doesn't matter to an individual accessing information whether that material is from NLNZ, another NZ source, or from anywhere else in the world. It may also not be clear whether the information is from a reliable source or authoritative or free to use. To address these issues, I encourage NLNZ to - include an international element to the strategic direction - add 'digital literacy' to the literacy and access goals for our next generation nation of readers.

How does the National Library’s draft strategic directions fit with your organisation’s priorities?

Museums Aotearoa shares with N:NZ and other GLAM sector organisations the broad goals of collecting, preserving and researching our cultural and natural heritage, the products of our creative individuals and makers, and ensuring our communities (and the world) have access to and ongoing engagement with them. We are all kaitiaki for future generations, and our mandate is to work 'in the service of society'. Museums Aotearoa priorities include collaboration with any other organisation or individual which will help us to provide more public value and wider impact.

In which areas are you or your organisation interested in partnering with or contributing to the National Library?

We are particularly interested in: - digitization of collections to enhance public access - cross-sector collaboration to make best use of capacity and resources - breaking down silos and barriers between different agencies and the public we all serve

How would you like to be involved as the strategic directions are progressed?

As a potential partner

Any other thoughts you'd like to share?

I applaud the thoughtful, varied, engaged and transparent ways in which NLNZ has approached this strategic exercise, and look forward to continuing the process.

Services to Schools Capability Services Team

Submitted on 31/08/2016 4:42pm on

What sector do you work in?

National Library staff

How often do you interact with the National Library of New Zealand?

Frequently

Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030? 

Yes

Why or why not?

At a general level the S2S Capability Services Team is supportive of the three themes. They represent the (actual and potential) role of the National Library in its broadest sense, incorporating people, collections (taonga), connections (access), story, and technology. They also provide a line of sight with the broader priorities of DIA, specifically in relation to building stronger communities, providing access to trusted information, and transformed service delivery. The themes and key terms do need to be defined specifically in order for them to become meaningful (that is, more than aspirational ‘catchy slogans’). For example ‘words’ – are these written words only? Spoken? English language only? ‘Taonga’ – what about the non-word based treasures in the collection? It would also be helpful if the integrated nature of the themes was more explicitly articulated. How do they reflect and support each other to really create an information and knowledge rich National Library that truly adds value to New Zealand?

Do the strategic directions fit with your view of the role of a national library?

Yes

Why or why not?

Yes. The role of a National Library is to be more than simply a repository (whether digital or print). Collection items must be made accessible and available, supplemented by services to build literacy and knowledge in all New Zealanders, connecting them with their culture and heritage and contributing to a nation that is well-informed, understanding, and prosperous. The themes are also future-focused and broad enough to encompass valuable work in a variety of specific areas. For any ‘national’ library there is by definition a specific role related to nation-wide leadership. Generally the document aspires to this with broad priorities and initiatives, the desire to develop partnerships and work in collaboration with others (acknowledging complementary strengths), and an emphasis on systems.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Network connection linking digital content across all sectors

Why?

Horizon 3: Exploration and Transformation is considered the most important due to its potential for providing New Zealanders with more equitable access to information and knowledge. As described currently however, the Horizon is focused on information infrastructure and products. It is missing crucial aspects related to people’s abilities (skills, competencies, mental models, and multiple literacies). We need to strengthen the ability of people to access, understand, evaluate and use vast amounts of information in multiple formats. Services to Schools have an important role in building this type of capability within the education, library and literacy sectors. It is the combination of human capability and technology that truly allows for the development of partnerships, connection and “exploration and transformation” at an individual, community, national and international level.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

National contracts for licensing e-content and new systems

Why?

Horizon 1: Continuous Improvement is problematic mainly due to its lack of definition regarding ‘e-content’. If this term means ‘e-books’ then there are platform, copyright, and access issues that have thus far proved too complex to resolve in any general manner (particularly for schools). Horizon 2: Next Generation is theoretically positive but would be challenging to deliver. This is due to the lack of resourcing and capacity within the National Library to “deliver network solutions”, but also because of the initiative’s dependence on cooperation from such a diverse group of stakeholders. It may be difficult to get schools to engage in a shared library management system. This is due to resourcing and capacity issues as well as the ‘self-managing’ environment. Also any ‘school-friendly’ system would need a level of sophistication, enabling the integration of the NZ curriculum and individual teacher/school planning with collection items. Ideally the system should also integrate with accredited Student Management Systems. It is also important to remember that an integrated library management system on its own does not achieve equity. Capability and capacity issues in schools need to be considered and addressed in order to achieve this goal.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

Programmes to inspire reading & support literacy

Why?

Horizon 1: Continuous Improvement, specifically the use of S2S expertise and reach into schools to facilitate stronger reading engagement is crucial. This exists in a limited way however we are yet to reach all schools. Our approach needs to be strengthened and expanded at a national level. There is an opportunity to lead thinking and work in reading engagement. To do this we need to develop a compelling message, sharing effectively the impacts reading for pleasure can have on individuals and communities. This could involve undertaking research on impacts on student achievement, life outcomes, community wellbeing; the role of library in supporting this; as well as developing partnerships to drive nation-wide initiatives. Under this horizon the mention of “South Auckland literacy programmes targeting Pacific peoples’ literacy levels” is problematic. This appears to fall well outside of the National Library’s remit, for example the project seems too specific for a strategic directions document and lacks scoping, it is local rather than national, about literacy acquisition rather than reading engagement, and potentially caters for a niche already well served by others. We support the establishment of a New Zealand Children’s Laureate and the position’s stated purpose to champion the cause of reading.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A cross-sector partnership to improve literacy levels

Why?

The Horizon 2: Next Generation “promotion of reading engagement as a response to adult literacy challenges” is problematic. Depending on how exactly this is defined (for example via specific programmes like that in South Auckland discussed under Horizon 1) this is a new and significant area of work for the National Library, and likely to require careful planning, the development of partnerships (with for example adult literacy groups, adult education providers, workplace education providers) and significant resource. Such an initiative would necessarily need to involve public libraries. There are still populations in New Zealand that are not close to a public library. How do we provide equitable access to programmes for isolated communities? In the first instance it would seems sensible and more effective to leverage existing S2S expertise, resources and relationships to strengthen the focus on children, young people and their school communities. If well done this could have a long term effect, and may eventually impact on adult literacy levels. Related to this is the need for a shared vision for school libraries and an understanding of their crucial role in promoting reading engagement.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

Digitisation and distribution of te reo Māori published content

Why?

Horizon 2: Next Generation is the most important, both in regards to the proposed increased emphasis on te reo Māori collections; and the preservation and accessibility of the National Library’s documentary heritage collections. The National Library is an important repository. Through community partnerships, digitisation, and technology we have opportunities to strengthen institutional and individual access to Māori language content, and heritage items. This will support the continued sustainability of te reo Maori as a living language; share National Library expertise; and establish a platform/framework enabling others (including institutions and future generations) to interact with content. It is interesting to consider how these items might be made accessible and relevant to New Zealand’s increasingly diverse population. S2S also has a particular interest in making items of these kinds accessible and appropriate for schools and students.

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A greater diversity of people supported & inspired to create & share our diverse heritage

Why?

In spite of the acknowledgement of the overall diversity of NZ’s general population, it would appear that essentially the focus is bicultural. This needs to be clarified, is the objective to embrace cultural diversity but with a focus still on the national languages? If so this raises issues regarding what it means to be a ‘national’ library in an increasingly diverse New Zealand context, how, for example, can ‘other’ New Zealanders access resources in their own languages (including resources for reading for pleasure). The proposed collaboration between New Zealand and Pacific institutions is important but also has challenges. These are in regards to capacity and resourcing, as well as different paradigms regarding preservation. This would be a significant piece of work and would likely require dedicated funding.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

In regards to the One Knowledge Network and an increasing focus on digital, what are we doing to address the digital divide? How will the National Library support and provide equitable access to those who don’t have the skills, budget, or knowledge to connect with our content? Are we leaving a significant group of citizens behind? Work in the areas of open access to government information and Creative Commons licensing would also be of value. Services to Schools may have a role here in regards to capability building in schools. Within the National Library how are staff conceptualised? Are they considered a critical success factor and if so how will they be supported, developed and resourced to deliver on the National Library’s vision?

How does the National Library’s draft strategic directions fit with your organisation’s priorities?

Excellent alignment with Services to Schools strategic priorities.

In which areas are you or your organisation interested in partnering with or contributing to the National Library?

Contribution to further thinking, development of frameworks and initiatives, operationalizing initiatives.

How would you like to be involved as the strategic directions are progressed?

As a potential partner

Any other thoughts you'd like to share?

Jackie Dennis on behalf of the New Zealand Society of Authors (PEN NZ Inc)

Submitted on 31/08/2016 4:58pm

What sector do you work in?

Literature sector

How often do you interact with the National Library of New Zealand?

Frequently

Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030? 

Yes

Why or why not?

Yes, with one reservation. Authors are missing from your Draft Strategic Direction except when mentioned in context of encouraging diversity. Authors create the substance of the National Library collections, whether in the form of published works or authors' research papers. It is the authors that create the sequence of words that share knowledge, engage readers and enrich cultural life in New Zealand.

Do the strategic directions fit with your view of the role of a national library?

Yes

Why or why not?

Yes, to share knowledge, encourage reading from cradle to grave, and to value and protect the national library collections (largely made up or words, created by authors) are good strategic directions for the national library. However we would like to see more acknowledgement of, and compensation to authors for the use of their works.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

National contracts for licensing e-content & new systems

Why?

The digital publishing transition cannot happen until there are licenses in place for the reproduction of copyrighted works. If the library is going to lend the works, there needs to be a mechanism in place for financial remuneration to authors for the loaning of the books and research papers that are not in the public domain.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

National contracts for licensing e-content and new systems

Why?

Getting agreements in place for the digitised copies of the works to be made available over the internet will take time and negotiation. The current PLR system is based on number of copies held by the library and this will not be functional for digitised copies. Either the legislation for PLR is updated and based on number of times a work is loaned, or a new mechanism is put in place for the distribution of digitised content. Breaking new ground is challenging as acknowledged in Marianne Takle's paper "The Norwegian National Digital Library"

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

NZ Children’s Laureate appointed to champion reading

Why?

Reading from an early age increases literacy levels, and a NZ Children's Laureate will champion reading from an early age, therefore a NZ Children's Laureate will help increase literacy levels.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

N/A

Why?

N/A

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

Shared expertise to acquire, curate & preserve our documentary heritage & make it accessible

Why?

Technology is not the future, it is now. We need to keep pace with other countries in the digital arena.

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

A sustainable, fit-for-purpose digital preservation approach to manage new digital content

Why?

There are three aspects to digitisation - preserving works, managing / administering works and accessing works. If digitised works are to be loaned there needs to be a mechanism in place to ensure authors / creators are paid if the work is still in copyright. Also, the administration of PLR could be improved as part of the library's digital shift, for example payments being made on actual holdings rather than estimates taken from surveys.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

The next draft of the National Library strategic direction should state that removing barriers to share knowledge will be done in a way that retains the rights of copyright owners and authors. Removing such barriers without considering the authors will have a negative impact on innovation by further reducing the ability of authors (national library content creators) to make a viable living. Sometimes ‘users’ are given precedence over the creators and this should not happen at our National Library. We support the driver Words as taonga BUT this concept must recognise and respect the writers and creators.

How does the National Library’s draft strategic directions fit with your organisation’s priorities?

One of our organisations priorities is to ensure writers are fairly paid for their work. It is unclear if your proposed strategic response around knowledge, reading and words considers the authors of those words

In which areas are you or your organisation interested in partnering with or contributing to the National Library?

We are keen to partner with the National Library to create opportunities for writers and encourage greater diversity of writers. We would also like to contribute to discussions around the improved administration of PLR, and any planning on how to compensate authors when digitised books and research papers are made availability for loan.

How would you like to be involved as the strategic directions are progressed?

As a potential partner

Any other thoughts you'd like to share?

Thank you for the opportunity to give feedback on your Draft Strategic Directions.

For example we’d expect to see exhibitions/presentations (we have contributed to several in the past, including ‘One More Acre’.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

It would be useful to have the scope of the document more clearly stated, for example, does it incorporate the Alexander Turnbull Library? If so, are the collections in the ATL covered: oral history, music, sound? We ask because these are overlapping collection areas to our own. It does not include anything related to decisions around collection development, although ‘words, sounds and pictures’ are defined as ‘documentary heritage’ on p.8.

How does the National Library’s draft strategic directions fit with your organisation’s priorities?

It is difficult to see how or where our organisation’s priorities fit with this draft strategic document, as we can see no reference to our overlapping areas of collection development (see above), or specifically to the need for partnerships with us in areas outside digital preservation and rights (we’d like to add in exhibitions / presentations, as above).

Treaty Partnerships, Iwi Engagement, Audiovisual Collection Development and Management, Expertise, Digitisation Expertise, Exhibitions/Presentations.

As a potential partner

Do you think the 3 broad themes outlined (One knowledge network, A nation of readers, Words as taonga) are the right areas of focus for the National Library looking towards 2030?

Why or why not?

It is difficult to answer yes/no to this question because of what is missing in this document. Too much is invisible. The lack of commentary about the business as usual operations of the Library underestimates the current rate and nature of change within the Library, particularly in the digital space. For the proposed themes to be achieved a strong, trusted national library is essential but the document says nothing about what a national library should be or what the vision for the national library in 2030 is. There is a lack of stretch in many of the documented targets even in the H3 dimensions. Overall there is a lack of aspiration and no sense that the national library might be, for example, 'a place where a nation nourishes its memory and exerts its imagination – where it connects with its past and invents its future'. Collecting, being a repository for the nation's documentary heritage, supporting scholarship, national identity, civic procesess and democracy in a rapidly expanding digital world characterised by increased volumes, complexity and fragmentation/creation of new communities via new technologies/social media should be a core theme for the national library in 2030.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'One knowledge network' do you consider to be most important?

Redeveloped digital services for better interaction with National Library collections

Why?

Our response to an increasingly complex digital world will be a key determinant of our success by 2030. Our collecting and our collections will be predominantly digital by then. Our digitisation programme will be generating digital material for wide-spread dissemination that would not otherwise have occurred. Computational techniques are already available for interrogating large amounts of data. We need to be able to work with a wider range of communities to answer a wider range of questions. We need to add contextual, linguistic, etc analysis tools while continuing to enhance the search capabilities we already have. It is difficult to guess what digital will look like in 2030 but we do know that if we’re not responding to technological change it is likely that we will be failing to do our jobs.

Which of the proposed 'One knowledge network' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Connection of the NZ network linking digital content across all sectors

Why?

It is not clear what this means. What is the ‘NZ network’? What content? Is this more than NZ content? What sectors? What does linking mean? A national knowledge network is a genuine and meaningful target, but it is not clear that this is what is intended or what it might comprise. There is an opportunity here to set the bar much higher than currently proposed.

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'A nation of readers' do you consider to be most important?

Distribute te reo material through e-devices to te reo Māori learners

Why?

The more we can support reo and language revitalisation the better. A lot of potential in conjunction with Words as Taonga and Digitisation and Distribution of te reo Māori Published Content, although the specificity of ‘learners’ (when we want a nation of readers) and ‘published’ is already constraining.

Which of the proposed 'A nation of readers' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

Cross-sector commitment for positive outcomes through reading

Why?

The choices here are pretty much ‘working with’ activities. It is not clear how they would enhance current library offerings in this space. What is the real opportunity for the National Library in this space?

Which of the proposed ideas for success for 'Words as taonga' do you consider to be most important?

A sustainable, fit-for-purpose digital preservation approach to manage new digital content

Why?

I’m the Programme Director Preservation Research & Consultancy :-) More seriously, our collecting and our collections will be predominantly digital by 2030. Our digitisation programme will be generating digital material requiring long-term safekeeping. A responsive, evolving, fit-for-purpose digital preservation programme which can ensure the long-term safekeeping of the Library’s digital collections will be core to its ability to meet its mandates into the future and provide the bedrock for the ongoing fitness of the organisation as a trusted repository for the nation’s digital heritage. It also plays a key role in One Knowledge Network and A Nation of Readers as well as Words as Taonga.

Which of the proposed 'Words as taonga' ideas for success do you consider to be most problematic?

New National Library programmes encouraging learning about constitutional documents

Why?

This, and the auditorium, are approved, in-flight projects. Focus for 2030, even in Horizon 1, should be on new initiatives enhancing the reach of the Library such as Strengthened NZ & Pacific collections reflecting our changing population & culture.

What do you think is missing from the National Library’s draft strategic directions?

There is a lack of focus on digital. Even if the themes and the horizons stay the same, by 2030 everything we do in those contexts will be digital whether it’s the One Knowledge Network or A Nation of Readers or Words as Taonga. In order to meet the demands of the Positioning for the Future programme we need to have a better understanding of what digital means, how deeply it penetrates our business and how pervasive it will be by 2030. A specific piece of commissioned research around digital and the National Library might be in order as part of the current process, similar to that undertaken in 2003.
