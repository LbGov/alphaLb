Submitted 30/08/2016

Were you aware of the International Open Data Charter prior to reading about it here?Â 

Yes

Do you see value in NZ adopting the International Open Data Charter?Â 

Yes

If you see value in adopting the Charter, tell us why?

It would express New Zealand's commitment at a national level to a universal set of open data principles. The recommendation to Cabinet to approve adoption would need to clarify its relationship to New Zealand's 2011 Declaration on Open and Transparent Government and the 2011 NZ Data and Information Management Principles. The former relates only to high-value public data whereas the latter sets out principles for managing all government-held information, and is a fundamental document applied across the public service.

Were you aware of the NZ Data & Information Management Principles prior to reading about these here?

Yes

Do you see value in the current Principles?

Yes

If you see value in the current Principles, tell us why?Â 

They state NZ government's high-level commitment to quality management of all the information the government holds on behalf of the public and use language taken from related legislation, for example the Official Information Act 1984, and the Privacy Act 1993. Examples of government-held information in scope are paper, digital information, published and unpublished information, collections of information including images and other information resources such as soil samples held at the Ministry of Business Innovation and Employment. This scope is broader than that of the Open Data Charter, as recognised by specific statements in the Charter, for examples, at ODC Principle 2(f) and Principle 5(d). They update and simplify the earlier 1997 Policy Framework for Government Held Information, which was influenced by US Office of Budget Management principles for managing government held information. It would be useful to build on the excellent comparison of the ODC and NZDIMP by adding analysis of what is covered by NZDIMP but not in the ODC - ie, a reverse gap analysis. This would assist with decisions on appropriate statements for 2016 and beyond.

Adopt the Open Data Charter and keep the Information and Data Management Principles
