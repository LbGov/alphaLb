Rochelle Ferguson - Operations Manager for Companion Animal Branch of the New Zealand Veterinary Association | 16 August 2016

I have: (select all that apply)

concerns about dog attacks

What do you think is the biggest contributing factor to a dog attack?

Dog owner

Please explain why this is the biggest contributing factor:

This issue is highly complex with a large number of contributing factors, to single out one over others is far too simplistic. Contributing factors include the genetics, early rearing experience, health, and training of the dog, the owner’s attitudes, experience and reasons for owning a dog, the situations surrounding each attack, including the victim’s behavior, and society’s tolerance of what constitutes normal dog behaviour.

What do you think is the best way to reduce dog attacks? (select all that apply)

Require owners to complete dog obedience classes, Give councils more powers , Education about dog behaviour, Other

If you selected 'Other' as the best way to reduce dog attacks, please explain.

Given these multiple factors, it is clearly not just a dog problem. Any meaningful solution will require planned responses delivered across a wide range of agencies. These include local and central government, animal control personnel, the veterinary profession, the medical profession, dog trainers and behaviorists, educators, dog breeders, and dog welfare agencies.

Do you have any further suggestions to reduce the risk and harm of dog attacks?

The NZVA has developed a comprehensive position statement on the dangerous dog issue that involves a revised dog classification system, owner licensing, breeder registration, education, control of unwanted dogs and further empowering of dog enforcement authorities. We would welcome an opportunity to discuss this further with you.

How do you think dog owners can help to reduce attacks? (select all that apply)

Keep dogs on leash when in public , Muzzle dogs when in public, Owners and dogs must take obedience training , Be more considerate of other people and dogs in public, Properly fencing off property , Other

If you selected 'Other', please explain how dog owners can reduce attacks.

Dog owners must assume responsibility for their dog(s), this includes providing for their needs, socializing and training them to reduce aggression, selecting a type of dog that suits their circumstances. Muzzling may be required if a dog is not trustworthy and ensuring they have control over the dog both when it is on and off the lead. They must also ensure that a dog that is unfriendly to visitors is securely confined and unable to escape.

How do you think councils can help to reduce dog attacks? (select all that apply)

Establishing more spaces for exercising dogs , Patrolling the streets, Quicker responses to complaints , Punishing irresponsible dog owners

If you selected 'Other', please explain how councils can reduce attacks.

Councils should clearly outline what constitutes responsible dog ownership, and educate owners on their responsibilities. Responsible dog ownership should be supported with significant incentives within the registration system that encourages ongoing dog training, effective containment, microchipping and de-sexing. The aim of reducing dog attacks is compromised however, while a large pool of unregistered dogs exists. Dog control officers should be resourced to patrol and actively target these dogs, with penalties for an unregistered dog offense being applied to their full extent.

I think the government can help to reduce attacks by: (select all that apply)

Increasing public awareness about safety around dogs, Giving councils more powers, Requiring the police do more to enforce dog control law , Requiring dog owner licencing

If you selected 'Other', please explain how government can reduce attacks.

The existing classifications of “menacing” and “dangerous” do not adequately support successful dog control in New Zealand. Specifically, the automatic menacing classification given to dogs based on their breed. This is a gross oversimplification of the issue and fails to capture large numbers of potentially dangerous dogs that belong to other breeds or are crossbreds. It does not address or promote responsible dog ownership, and wastes resources by unnecessarily restricting both docile members of targeted breeds (Collier, 2006), and dogs that are not menacing but are classified as such simply on the ‘visual assessment’ of their breed. Under current legislation, dogs can also be classified as dangerous based on behaviour that ranges from “showing aggressive behaviour” through to being involved in a fatal attack. Once classified as dangerous this endures for the rest of the dog’s existence. We believe that the failure to take into account the context of aggressive behaviour hinders the ability of the legislation to effectively address dog aggression problems. A multi-tier classification system based on dog behaviour and not breed must be adopted to address the issues above. This system would also allow for consideration of the context in which the behaviour occurred, and support earlier identification of potentially dangerous dogs. If breed is to be used as criteria for classification, owners of dogs classified based on breed, must have recourse to having their dogs behavior assessed to determine if they are menacing or not. A revised classification system for dogs should also make provision for behavior testing by an independent authorised person to provide further integrity to the system. This will serve to reduce expenses associated with objections, and guard against unjustified euthanasia. Any classification system adopted should contain a rehabilitation pathway that involves training and behavior testing for adult dogs exhibiting less serious behaviours, to allow for the classification to be rescinded. This rehabilitation should be mandatory and at the expense of the owner. The legislative framework proposed by the Australian Veterinary Association is put forward as an example of the legislative framework. (Australian Veterinary Association Ltd, 2012). Dog ownership is a privilege and not a right, and in order to support this, owner licensing should be implemented through an amendment to the Dog Control Act (1996). The aim of licensing is to ensure all owners who obtain a dog are aware of the dog’s needs and the responsibilities associated with dog ownership. Obtaining a license would require attending an educational seminar on dog ownership and responsibilities and passing a test based on the course material. All dog license holders would receive education on their responsibility to use whatever appropriate methods of restraint are available to ensure public safety at all times for example leads, muzzles and fencing. Existing owners of dogs older than two years of age, with no dog related incidents on their record would automatically be grandfathered into the scheme. Penalties for owners of dogs that are involved in serious attacks could include loss of their dog license, as well as being fined and/or imprisoned as the law allows. Individuals who obtain a dog without a license should be fined, and the dog removed if a license cannot be obtained. While individuals may resent an increased hurdle to dog ownership, we believe that this will be mitigated by the significantly reduced public risk by promoting responsible attitudes towards dog ownership. Licensing owners will serve the wider community by reducing the personal and financial costs associated with dog aggression. Endorsements for classified dogs All owners of dogs that are classified as dangerous or menacing would be required to obtain an endorsement to their license that allows them to own a classified dog. The endorsement would require that owners are tested for their mental and physical abilities to handle the dog safely, be subject to a criminal background check, have the support from residents in the house, and have facilities which allow such a dog to be restrained at home and not be a danger to visitors. The dog should also be subject to behaviour testing and retraining. The endorsement would not be available to minors. If an endorsement is not obtained, or if the dog fails behavior testing then the classified dog should be surrendered. Dog population management Breeding Permits To successfully address dangerous dog issues, the unwanted dog population must also be managed. It is essential that dogs are not easily replaced by a new puppy if they are removed under this legislation. Owners of dogs capable of breeding should be required to hold a specific permit issued by the territorial authority. Exemptions could be made if the dog owner was a member of a breeding organisation that required its members to operate in accordance with a code of ethics. This recommendation is based on the observation that countries that rigorously apply breeding and ownership licensing, such as Switzerland and Sweden, do not have shelters that are overflowing in unwanted dogs. By limiting the supply of dogs, these countries have fostered an attitude where dogs are seen as “part of the family”, resulting in low abandonment rates (Tasker, 2007).
