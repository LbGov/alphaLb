How do we inspire the many diverse communities in New Zealand to participate, explore and use the record of government?

Engagement with diverse communities of users is a key challenge for all archival authorities. While better funded institutions may allocate resources to public programming and public engagement programs, not all institutions have that capacity. Other approaches could include partnering with community groups and other cultural institutions, working with volunteer networks, or enabling community feedback and participation to add value to the core catalogue of holdings.

How can archivists help people navigate government information, records and archives in a born-digital environment?

There is a clear public expectation, which translates into a political expectation, that in the 21st century government information should be digital. Government information is being created as digital content, however archival authorities still hold large collections of physical archives. Any strategy going forward has to address public and political expectations that government archives must be digital and that existing physical archives must be delivered digitally. This is a difficult narrative with expectations of digital access at odds with the rate of digitization (eg NAA having digitized only 4% of holdings over a decade) and the rate of transfer of born-digital archives (Archives New Zealand, PROV and SRANSW with only small holdings of born-digital archives transferred). There is a gap between expectation and reality which must be addressed through a principled and careful narrative with a clear outcome in mind. Archival authorities should also clearly articulate archival principles which address the context, evidential value and integrity of the record in order to help people navigate government archives. Archivists must present a coherent narrative to explain the purpose of an archival authority in plain language in order to address demands for use. Systems for controlling and providing access to information, records and archives in a born-digital environment should be as intuitive and accessible as archival principles will allow.

What are the pros and cons of government agencies setting their own access status classifications?

This question could be re-cast to ask “In what context and with what framework should government agencies make access decisions?” What is the role (is there a role) for Archives New Zealand in achieving consistency across government agencies with regard to access status of types of government records? In Western Australia government agencies have a legislative framework within which to assess the information they create and to classify the access status of that information. The SROWA assists the agency in developing their Retention and Disposal Schedule and the agency requests approval from the State Records Commission (Auditor General, Information Commissioner, Ombudsman and an information professional) with feedback from an advisory committee and with the recommended access status set using precedents established through specific legislation or based on similar records types in other government agencies or in other jurisdictions. The aim of the framework is to allow the agency to request the access status classification within a framework which provides a consistent, standardised approach.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if government interventions are based on data?

In a West Australian context, the SROWA requires agencies to meet minimum compliance requirements to safeguard the integrity of their digital records. Agencies are required to keep data and migrate it for as long as the data is needed (both in terms of business requirements and for long-term retention of critical data/archival data). The risk and the responsibility for the data in agency custody is with the agency. How an agency manages their data and the technical solutions they employ are in some ways up to the agency – however the agencies are required to manage their data to achieve minimum outcomes, including accessibility, migration and ongoing management of data.

Do you think Archives New Zealand should shift the focus of recordkeeping regulation to system design?

The assumption throughout the discussion document that “digital systems capability” can help address the strategic focus areas is sound. Similarly the statement “This will require checks and balances to be in place around privacy and security” is also fine. However systems and system designs change over time – especially given the discussion document is contemplating a forty year time-frame. Archival principles should endure and these should be applied by archival authorities in establishing functional requirements for recordkeeping systems. SROWA favours taking an active role in understanding recordkeeping/business systems however does not advocate designing one system or imposing a singles system design.

Should Archives New Zealand develop capability in-house to influence system design or work through others?

Both. The question is something of a false dichotomy. Perhaps a clear strategic statement should be that Archives New Zealand intends to address system design and that the organisation should develop the skill-set/capacity to address system design. The outcome over forty years will most likely be achieved through many combinations of in-house and external solutions. How this is achieved is not as important as what it is Archives New Zealand wants to achieve and that the organisation understands/has the appropriate expertise.

How prescriptive should Archives New Zealand be around recordkeeping standards (e.g. what should the balance be between voluntary standards versus mandatory requirements)?

Perhaps the question to consider first could be “What does Archives New Zealand/the New Zealand government want to achieve with standards?” Then consider can this be achieved through voluntary or mandatory requirements. Have other archival authorities in other jurisdictions set similar expectations, did they use voluntary or mandatory and have they achieved their outcomes? In a Western Australian context, mandatory standards are statements of principles with clear minimum compliance requirements. There is flexibility in how these minimum requirements are met, and SROWA provides advice, training and technical guidelines for agencies.

Those responsible for the administration of Archives NZ have no option but to be faced with the unenviable task of giving the highest priority to increased funding. It is unenviable because it may entail diverting funding from immediate demands of its functioning as a national archives. There will be many responses to this questionnaire detailing what, quite properly, should be done. Unfortunately few of the recommendations will be achievable without increased funding. My own responses should also be seen in this light.

How do we inspire the many diverse communities in New Zealand to participate, explore and use the record of government?

Developing strong outreach programmes which have the ability to educate and inspire both producers and potential users in the value and utility of public records -- not just those records which are non-current and are of permanent value. The programmes should be done in consultation with people who have expertise in public relations and delivered by people who have had PR training as well as the essentials of archival practices.

How can archivists help people navigate government information, records and archives in a born-digital environment?

By Archives NZ taking the initiative by having suitably trained outreach personnel actively out in the user community. User-tested guides, whether digital or hard-copy would also be necessary.

What barriers exist to Māori accessing and/or managing taonga and government information for cultural and economic benefit?

Broadly speaking the same barriers that exist in other parts of their life. Measures need to be taken which allow for cultural differences

What are the pros and cons of government agencies setting their own access status classifications?

It needs to be recognised that, with very few exceptions, the prime objectives of government agencies, is to perform the functions that enable them to achieve the objectives of the Acts or Regulations by which they operate. Rarely do these functions include providing access to records to people outside the agency. It is therefore unsurprising that matters such as making provision for outsiders' access has low, or even, negative priority. While it should be recognised that, for a period time, closed access is a necessity, it should also be recognised that, eventually, all records should be publicly available. The only way to resolve the issue is for it to be made mandatory that access is to be determined by consultation between an informed Chief Archivist and the CEO of that agency. Access would not be the only issue to be determined: there is also the issue of ensuring that records, which are not to be released for many years, should be kept in accommodation suitable for long term preservation. At least nine times out of ten, this means storage under the direct control of Archives NZ.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if government interventions are based on data?

Archives NZ must take the initiative of ensuring that agencies are constantly reminded of their archival responsibilities. This implies an active Archives NZ participation in the agencies record-keeping practices.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if there are new methods of government decision-making?

As above.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if agencies merge or cease to exist?

By formulating, and continually up-dating standard-operating-procedures for such events and reminding all agencies of same.

Should Archives New Zealand be able to set out, more precisely, what records are to be kept for the long term because there are significant gaps (archival silences) in the record of government?

The answer is obvious. If Archives NZ had had sufficient resources in the past to participate actively in the agencies record-keeping there would be far fewer gaps than there are now.

Do you think Archives New Zealand should shift the focus of recordkeeping regulation to system design?

Should do both.

Should Archives New Zealand develop capability in-house to influence system design or work through others?

Without in-house capability to influence system design Archives NZ will not be able to fulfil its responsibilities. Only when it has this capability will it be able to decide to what degree it will work through others.

How prescriptive should Archives New Zealand be around recordkeeping standards (e.g. what should the balance be between voluntary standards versus mandatory requirements)?

Archives New Zealand should be fully prescriptive -- after informed and detailed consultation with the agencies.

Any other thoughts you’d like to share?

Sweden has one of the oldest and most effective archives and records systems in the world. Yet for at least a couple of hundred years it operated without archives legislation. The reason it functioned so well, however, was because Sweden has long operated an effective Freedom of Information system. Effective FOI can operate only when you have an effective public records systemm
