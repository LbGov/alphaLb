How do we inspire the many diverse communities in New Zealand to participate, explore and use the record of government?

Tell stories that relate to the communities and that people can have a personal connection to (and contribute to). For example use social media channels and develop a strong public programme (both online and in the 'real world'). Ensure that information/ archives can be found easily, used and re-used.

How can archivists help people navigate government information, records and archives in a born-digital environment?

1. Ensure that archives are clearly described, the context and provenance are accurate and meaningful (using appropriate archival descriptive practice - to maintain the 'formal' record - what ever it is or may become in the future). 2. Create an environment and archival management system (or other solution) that will allow communities and individuals to enhance and contribute to the description of archives (to optimise discoverability and re use) - e.g. crowd sourcing...etc.

What barriers exist to Māori accessing and/or managing taonga and government information for cultural and economic benefit?

Access channels, archival systems (currently ARCHWAY) and descriptive information included in the system do not adequately reflect or support a Maori view of knowledge or knowledge sharing. For example it is not clear where taonga/ information related to different groups (such as iwi or hapu...etc) exist within the Archival holdings. Where it is known it is not easy to add this information to the system - for staff or for 'users' (e.g. crowd sourcing to enhance descriptive information and tagging). There is also a limited resource of staff at Archives New Zealand with appropriate knowledge, experience and expertise to enhance records retrospectively. Because transferring public offices do not (and probably should not?) have a requirement to add descriptive information of high value to Maori as a part of the current transfer process, the responsibility to enhance these records (to unlock their full value for everyone) belongs to Archives New Zealand (post transfer). Also, the current physical environment, services and approach used to engage in archival research do not meet the needs of Maori for various reasons. Archives New Zealand needs to make changes to foster a greater awareness of Te Ao Maori into modern archival practice and radically assess the way access is delivered (on site and online) so that they can actively engage with the wider Maori community in meaningful and sustainable ways.

What are the pros and cons of government agencies setting their own access status classifications?

This is a tricky one .... it depends what the role of the public office is, what the records are and if they choose to identify the records as open or closed access. I think it is probably more beneficial to stick with the current regime where 1. public office identifies what they think the access status should be and 2. access status is reviewed by a group of people (in consultation with the public office). Then the final decision is made (currently by the Chief Archivist). It is probably best for an external body to approve access status as this would hopefully support transparency and better trust in government information in the long term....

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if government interventions are based on data?

Not sure about this one - the key is to ensure that information is discoverable, understandable, and accessible long term. Identifying clearly what is important information and which public offices (or functions of government) are generating important information. Then these 'functions' or the public office/s involved in generating that information could be more closely supported or regulated (in particular with regard to how data is being generated, shared and re-used). A solution to identifying who is accountable or responsible for maintaining this information is an issue that would need to addressed, (particularly in the context of collaborative work for example).

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if there are new methods of government decision-making?

See comment above

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if agencies merge or cease to exist?

See comment above

Should Archives New Zealand be able to set out, more precisely, what records are to be kept for the long term because there are significant gaps (archival silences) in the record of government?

Yes. It is quite controversial to suggest this approach, and is goes against traditional archival practice/ theory. However, there would be a benefit in developing an acquisition policy (much like a collections policy - which I know is a naughty thing to say). I would see an acquisition policy sitting along side a more traditional appraisal process, where the public office identifies the 'business' value and retention periods for their records as a part of standard appraisal - which would most likely in the future (say 10 - 20 years time for example) be an activity incorporated in to system design to allow for automated sentencing/ disposal/ transfer. The second phase of 'appraisal' using Archives New Zealand's acquisition policy could take place at this time. The acquisition policy would view the records through an 'archival lens' and presumably would be focussed on records that are of high 'cultural' or societal value, records that may in future may protect the rights and entitlements of the people...for example. I could see this working better than the current system of 'collecting' that we have in place.

Do you think Archives New Zealand should shift the focus of recordkeeping regulation to system design?

I think it should do both but focus on high risk/ value functions of government. E.g. - It depends on the function the public office and the type of information they are generating. 1. Identify which functions or public offices are generating 'high value/ high risk' information. 2. Regulate the development and implementation of the systems (at the point of the systems being designed) 3. Monitor how the systems are being used and the quality of the information being generated/ managed using the system. People create, use and manage information...it's a symbiotic relationship between systems/ tools and people, this is why you need to regulate both recordkeeping/ information management and system design.

Should Archives New Zealand develop capability in-house to influence system design or work through others?

Both. In house - digital preservation in particular - this team should be able to advise and collaborate with Public Offices. System design - regulate and support/ advise.

How prescriptive should Archives New Zealand be around recordkeeping standards (e.g. what should the balance be between voluntary standards versus mandatory requirements)?

Again it depends on what the function of the Public Office is and what type of information they are generating. I'd probably lean more towards having mandatory standards (that are realistically implementable) and have less of them. Then identify the Public Offices that are generating very high value/ high risk records and more actively regulate or support? these sectors.

Any other thoughts you’d like to share?

We have questions on each strategic focus area, but if you want to provide a general comment only, please do so here.

The general strategy is very positive. The three focus areas create huge potential for moving forward. It is important that national records are recognised as "the most precious" of our assets. These national assets in fact tell the New Zealand story, and therefore crucial to capture information. Leadership and regulations are required to ensure key areas are not missing.

How do we inspire the many diverse communities in New Zealand to participate, explore and use the record of government?

Better promotion needed, as many people do not know much about Archives NZ. A wider understanding of the importance of holding and accessing national assets would lead to wider usage. Promotion material that could be used by other agencies would be useful. Need to raise the awareness at an earlier age, by sharing some of the NZ stories, capture NZ tales, road shows to schools. Connect to other networks for wider promotion and usage (e.g. SMART library network http://smartlibraries.org.nz/). It must be user friendly - easy to use, easy to find information, easy to read (pitch for all ages).

How can archivists help people navigate government information, records and archives in a born-digital environment?

A framework that is EASY to use - i.e. to capture/save content, to access/search/find information. Create an environment that people are confident they can easily find what they are looking for. Use technology (e.g. social media) to engage with all age groups.

What barriers exist to Māori accessing and/or managing taonga and government information for cultural and economic benefit?

Need to bridge the digital divide. Encourage non-government organisations and iwi to share national assets. Raise awareness of the value thereof, and also reach out to younger audiences to capture and promote "the NZ story". Consult Maori experts. Government to provide funding for resources and education to not only promote to, but to allow remote locations to be able to tap into the "digital' environment.

What are the pros and cons of government agencies setting their own access status classifications?

Better if external agencies set their own access status classifications. It would better support transparency (open to sharing in own environment) and engagement (easier to maintain as it is more familiar), and ultimately increase wider engagement and trust in government information. On the negative side, it will create more inconsistency, which means it is more difficult and expensive to administer.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if government interventions are based on data?

This is tricky. Data sets generally contain sensitive information which are not shared publically. It is important though that information is discoverable. Source of the truth information, outlining data sources and contact details for further information should be available to ensure information is accessible long term. This should contain Migration Plans to ensure information from legacy systems are captured.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if there are new methods of government decision-making?

As above

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if agencies merge or cease to exist?

Processes should already be established whereby information is captured on an on-going basis. Should agencies merge, cease to exist or names change, mechanisms need to be put in place to capture this information, and feed into/link to Search to ensure all information is picked up. Migration plans should also be captured to allow for information of legacy systems not be lost.

Should Archives New Zealand be able to set out, more precisely, what records are to be kept for the long term because there are significant gaps (archival silences) in the record of government?

Archives NZ need to play a stronger regulatory role. This would require clear legislation, principles, standards, guidelines and support, including clear and simple GDA, and Retention & Disposal, and appraisal process. Government will also need to provide support (funding) to enable organisations to set up systems to enable this to happen. Archival silences often occur where information is of a commercially sensitive nature (e.g. tenders, or business solutions) - educate organisations on how to deal with this (e.g. include clause in contract that "at the date of transfer, the business sensitive information is no longer relevant", thus allowing information to be made public).

Do you think Archives New Zealand should shift the focus of recordkeeping regulation to system design?

Archives NZ need input into, but not focus on system design. Need to provide clear requirements for capturing information, i.e. what and how, so organisations can set up their own systems/processes to easily transfer information. Regulations and monitoring is needed to ensure this happens, and ultimately helps organisations in their "struggle".

Should Archives New Zealand develop capability in-house to influence system design or work through others?

Both. In-house to support, capture and maintain information in a manner that it is retained and accessible long term. Consult technical expertise to be stay on top of technical changes and advise/support.

How prescriptive should Archives New Zealand be around recordkeeping standards (e.g. what should the balance be between voluntary standards versus mandatory requirements)?

Needs to be a balance. Although mandatory standards are needed, they need to be clear, easy to follow and doable. There needs to be some flexibility to allow organisations to retain what is "important for the business" as well. If too restrictive, you risk engagement. if not restrictive enough, you risk gaps (archival silences) and losing important aspects of the NZ story.

Any other thoughts you’d like to share?

New standards are easy to follow and provides a mechanism to drive accountability.

We have questions on each strategic focus area, but if you want to provide a general comment only, please do so here.

Many statements in the Discussion Document are open to several interpretations and there is a lot of specialist language. This is a barrier to engaging with people who are less familiar with the language of the sector. All New Zealanders should be able to read and understand the intentions of an institution such as Archives New Zealand.

How do we inspire the many diverse communities in New Zealand to participate, explore and use the record of government?

Outreach needs to be specifically resourced. The UK National Archives recent released their strategy consultation document which included case studies. This would be an ideal way of demonstrating the kind of value that different communities can gain from Archives. The evidential value of government records in Treaty claims would be a good example. Much could be made of connecting Archives holdings with the school curriculum, perhaps taking advantage of National Library’s Pond to provide easy access to digitised resources. At a physical level, the current building is not well suited to exhibition or education spaces, but being able to physically connect with a collection is important, and makes both the holdings and the institution more real and relevant to people.

How can archivists help people navigate government information, records and archives in a born-digital environment?

Staff need to have a range of skills, particularly around education, technology, outreach and social media. Introducing alternate skill sets and paradigms about information is important, and not the sole domain of the Archivist. Making digital content findable directly from Google (rather than having to come through Archway), will make a significant difference. Although New Zealand is probably too small to implement major Crowdsourcing initiatives, providing options for people to add relevant metadata is crucial to improve findability, and to fill in gaps in what we know about a record. A good example would be the ability to add a range of Maori metadata or subject headings. Most paper records currently have very little data available to help people identify what the record is. As a result, we need to rethink who can contribute towards the description of archives and how they do that.

What barriers exist to Māori accessing and/or managing taonga and government information for cultural and economic benefit?

In terms of access, description is a major barrier. In general the archiving and recordkeeping communities are not skilled in appropriately describing or adding relevant metadata to records of interest to Māori. Assistance in what to implement in terms of Māori metadata for public records would be a forward step. Archives New Zealand, being an institution of Government, will be a barrier to some. Trust and investment in relationship building is key.

What are the pros and cons of government agencies setting their own access status classifications?

While agencies know their own information, they are likely to restrict information for too long a period. Much of what is currently restricted needs to be reviewed. Agencies need more guidance on valid reasons for restriction, in the same way that there are a limited number of reasons for restricting information under the Official Information Act. Otherwise access restrictions will be more restrictive than Official Information Act provisions.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if government interventions are based on data?

It’s unclear what the problem to be solved is in this question. Is the issue with the preservation of the data, the retention of what is deemed to be “important” data sets, or the capture of data at a point in time? Data extracts and calculations are already often being kept by agencies, as part of the context of the record that they produce. This practice should be continued and encouraged. Guidance on keeping this as part of the record would be useful. Data extracts and analysis may need to be distinguished from “calculations” as defined in GDA7 (1.6) if the expectation is that they should be preserved. As with preservation formats for other kinds of records, some degree of prescription around suitable data formats for taking point in time snapshots or data extracts would be useful.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if there are new methods of government decision-making?

The answer very much depends on the method of decision making, which is difficult to predict. If the methods involve new kinds of systems for data collection, then (as a high value data source) recordkeeping requirements need to be built in. An additional approach would be to look at skills development across the recordkeeping workforce around data collection and extraction, harvesting, format migration, preservation, open access, and security, to enable the workforce to make good decisions about how to handle this.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if agencies merge or cease to exist?

Signing off the discharge or transfer of records should be part of the responsibilities of the administrative head/s of the office, as part of the merger or disestablishment of the agency or agencies. An Archives staff member should be assigned to work with the agency early on, with a focus on transferring all eligible records to Archives under their existing schedule. Ideally, this would include a way for agencies to transfer their digital records. Bear in mind that often staff with the institutional knowledge about the records can leave quickly in change situations, so early intervention is important to identify priorities for records.

Should Archives New Zealand be able to set out, more precisely, what records are to be kept for the long term because there are significant gaps (archival silences) in the record of government?

Yes. This should be done as soon as possible.

Do you think Archives New Zealand should shift the focus of recordkeeping regulation to system design?

It’s unclear what is meant by system design in this question. The statements in the Discussion Document imply design of technical systems. If the aim of this focus area is for recordkeeping to be as simple as possible through quality design of technical systems, then a wider definition of what this looks like should be embraced. Implementing quality systems may include change management, user experience, automated classification, cloud-based systems, governance and exit strategies. There is also a need to think about a larger technical landscape – how could major technology players help us develop the next generation of information management systems or tools? It should be remembered also that at the technical level there are still numerous issues around how to handle legacy systems and email archives that need to be addressed. A more useful strategic focus would be on system-wide change across the entire Information landscape of Government. This includes looking at all the agencies, standards and laws involved in information and data management to gain a whole-of-system view. Aligning the disparate parts of the system is likely to require significant changes to ensure that the kinds of information that we want to be able to keep and access in 40 years is well managed and described now. There is still a role for recordkeeping regulation – without managing the here and now, we will not collect the records we want to be looking at in 2057.

Should Archives New Zealand develop capability in-house to influence system design or work through others?

Both in-house and external capability will be needed. Archives needs to employ people with practical experience who can facilitate and coordinate the work of others at the system level. This may require upskilling staff, hiring in, and bringing skills into the country. It seems unlikely that Archives would ever have the budget to sustain an entirely in-house capability long-term, given the numbers and level of specialist expertise that may be required, and the scarcity of those specialists in New Zealand.

How prescriptive should Archives New Zealand be around recordkeeping standards (e.g. what should the balance be between voluntary standards versus mandatory requirements)?

It should be 100% clear which standards (and aspects of those standards) are mandatory, and for which kind of organisation. Mandatory standards need to take into account the type of organisation to which the standards are being applied. It may not be reasonable to expect a small rural school to adhere to exactly the same recordkeeping standards as a large Government Organisation. However, only make mandatory those standards that will support the outcomes that Archives and its stakeholders are looking for. Some additional standards (such as formats suitable for long-term preservation) should be made mandatory in order to ensure that records can survive to 2057.

Any other thoughts you’d like to share?

At a strategic level: - There is little mention of Archives role to support agencies, except as a regulator. Information professionals in agencies are both customers and advocates for the work of Archives. Inadequate support to information professionals will make the strategic focus areas much harder to achieve. - Partnerships with other agencies or organisations with similar goals do not feature in the discussion document. These will be needed to fully achieve the strategic focus areas. - There is very little mentioned about how Archives will exercise its leadership role, as mentioned in sections 11(1)a and 11(g) of the Act. Many of the issues that the sector faces, particularly around access and preservation are difficult, but investment and leadership is crucial. - The strategy should reaffirm the importance of the independence of the Chief Archivist. The Chief Archivist is a role that contributes significantly to the accountability and transparency of government. - Given the forward looking nature of this document, it should be considered how relevant it is to still discuss the “record” in the digital age as something distinct from information or data. At an operational level: - While a forward focus is good, information professionals are still tackling a legacy of pre-PRA systems that cannot easily be classified and disposed of. Some assistance with addressing legacy issues is required so that we can move forward unencumbered. - With the rate of software format change, 25 years to transfer electronic records is too long. This needs to be rethought, or a different approach (such as access and management in place at the agency) needs to be designed.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if agencies merge or cease to exist?

In response to all “How do we ensure important information remains accessible…” questions: I’m not so sure decentralisation will have long term benefits, it’s quite hard to get people to follow standards and the more information we create the more out of control its going to get. The opposite may have better effect - centralise government information management systems (possibly a controversial idea, but I’m sure it could work with the right thinking and investment). From a point in time, NZ government and all its agencies could use one central system for BAU document and records management that includes records and archives management and public access capabilities. In such a system all agencies would be responsible for getting information in it and using it for day-to-day business; central government records staff can monitor and support its BAU use and ensure retention and disposal is consistently applied (transfer to Archives NZ could be within the system instead of across systems); and Archives NZ (DIA) can be responsible for maintenance of the system and making sure the information in it can be accessed and understood for as long as its needed. One system would make life a lot easier; no more procurement of systems, transferring, migrating, or closing of systems to worry about; just storage, disposal, a massive file plan, heaps of metadata to maintain, and provision of access. If agencies merge or cease to exist, the information is captured already, so it’s just a case of managing it. Possibly quite ambitious, but I see this working better then decentralisation for various reasons.

Would be easier to do as its only one system to control, and you’ll be able to monitor what agencies are doing in that one system. Government regulation will then be more focussed and straightforward. So both are required really.

How prescriptive should Archives New Zealand be around recordkeeping standards (e.g. what should the balance be between voluntary standards versus mandatory requirements)?

Every element of records management that has some risk attached to it should have prescriptive mandatory requirements, e.g. risk of the loss of information, inappropriate access, loss of context, etc. If there is no risk, then the standards could be relaxed somewhat – to best practice guidance.

Any other thoughts you’d like to share?

The opening section of the discussion document states: “Currently we have a role as a regulator of the Public Records Act 2005; a custodian role to care for and protect the records we hold; and a facilitating role around access to the information we hold. Yes, part of “wider archival sector”, but also part of government and subject to the requirements of the Public Records Act. The record of local government should have consideration in the 2057 vision, especially regarding system requirements, storage of records/archives and service delivery. The local government sector would benefit from more regulation in this space as local authority records management is extremely inconsistent across the country. Archives New Zealand needs to make it clear where they stand re: regulation for local government recordkeeping. A question that comes up often is “will you audit us?”, if there is to be no auditing can there at least be plans to put other mechanisms in place to ensure local authorities remain accountable for their records management practice. One solution: Archives New Zealand could enable local authorities to become “qualified/accredited” in management of records and archives, with those not meeting qualifications being required to become “qualified/accredited” within a certain timeframe (with support from Archives NZ). Level of qualification/accreditation required and time taken to get there could differ depending on the size of the council, responsibilities, and the type/quantity of records/archives held by the council… See: http://www.nationalarchives.gov.uk/archives-sector/archive-service-accreditation/ for inspiration. Could be a programme of work initially, to assess all Local Authorities, then an ongoing maintenance programme could become BAU.
