How do we inspire the many diverse communities in New Zealand to participate, explore and use the record of government?

Outreach - trained, archivally literate speakers talking to different user groups - speakers who know how to adapt the message for different groups. Getting to know the diverse communities. Taking the initiative, going to groups not waiting for them to come to you - developing a programme of visits. Developing guides in multiple formats, languages and presentations the diverse communities can relate to.

How can archivists help people navigate government information, records and archives in a born-digital environment?

Using systems that are straightforward to navigate - using users for testing before going live. Developing more tools that explain how archives are arranged and how to access them - particularly those that are not yet digitised - tools such as the administrative histories of government departments. There is useful information in appraisal reports that users of those records would find of assistance. Employment of more trained archivists who know and understand the records they manage and are therefore able to assist users.

What barriers exist to Māori accessing and/or managing taonga and government information for cultural and economic benefit?

Barriers are generally barriers for all not specifically for Maori - need for good communication, good descriptions of records, clear understanding of any access restrictions, systems that have been user-tested and are easy to use.

What are the pros and cons of government agencies setting their own access status classifications?

Pro: Agencies are perhaps more aware of sensitivity of information BUT Cons: agencies do not usually see the big picture - do not necessarily understand the longer term use of records beyond the reasons for which they were originally created. A tendency to want to protect information rather than make it available.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if government interventions are based on data?

Question is rather obscure. It must be accepted by agencies that ANZ has the ultimate control. This is more difficult with the current status of the Chief Archivist. There is a need for close involvement with records keepers but also for there to be high level (CEO) meetings. Provisions are there in the Act - they should be enforced.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if there are new methods of government decision-making?

ANZ's role is to ensure records are still created and that agencies accept and implement good records keeping practices. ANZ must work with agencies to understand what is happening in the decision-making arena and planning accordingly.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if agencies merge or cease to exist?

All of the questions in this section come back to the role ANZ plays with agencies. There must be an understanding of what is happening with mergers and closures, and a recognition that the records may be taken over as of right by ANZ. If a private company is involved provision must be made for continuing access to records and the eventual deposit with ANZ. A process should be developed to ensure systems superseded by mergers or closures are taken over by ANZ.

Should Archives New Zealand be able to set out, more precisely, what records are to be kept for the long term because there are significant gaps (archival silences) in the record of government?

ANZ can already set this out - it is part of the Public Records Act, records can only be destroyed with ANZ's approval. The Act also specifies that records are to be created. This is where close liaison with agencies becomes imperative.

Do you think Archives New Zealand should shift the focus of recordkeeping regulation to system design?

These two work hand in hand - regulation has to be there but it needs to work alongside system design but design should not drive retention decisions. ANZ needs to have cross government knowledge, the ability to advise but also to be trusted to advise knowledgeably, to build up connections with agencies, to have staff who are able to talk credibly with agencies about systems and who are seen as useful, credible advisers.

Should Archives New Zealand develop capability in-house to influence system design or work through others?

Both are needed - not either/or. Without in-house capability and a knowledge of what is being developed outside it would be less easy for ANZ to be credible when working with agencies.

How prescriptive should Archives New Zealand be around recordkeeping standards (e.g. what should the balance be between voluntary standards versus mandatory requirements)?

The setting of standards by professionals is imperative. Voluntary standards are extremely difficult to police. However there should also be some degree of flexibility to cater for unusual circumstances.

Any other thoughts you’d like to share?

Devolution of access to agencies is fraught with problems. Providing access to their records is not what agencies are set up for - it is not a core activity. They have neither the reading room space nor the staff to handle inquiries. Generally agencies are not particularly open in their release of information - having worked in departments handling OIA requests it becomes apparent that less is best. Changing this would be an uphill battle. For researchers having to visit each agency to access material would be time consuming and frustrating. With material held by ANZ much can be achieved in one place. An "all of government" system may help to overcome such issues if ANZ had automatic access to it and if it was set so restrictions could prevent sensitive material being seen. It is disappointing that there is little elaboration on and no questions about relating to the wider communities - local bodies, community archives etc. A feel for how ANZ is going to manage this part of its mandate under the Act would have been useful. Funding for managing the electronic future and employing staff to ensure these records are created may also be an issue. It is important that this exercise raises these questions in such a way that funding is adequately addressed.

The discussion document contains complex concepts and a lot of industry-specific terminology that many people would struggle to understand without an industry background. Ideally this document would be written so that it could be understood by a general audience. The interests of all sectors need to be represented and all information management roles ought to understand that this document is applicable and relevant to them. For example the term 'archives' is narrowly understood. An alternative might be to refer to 'information' and 'information management' as opposed to 'archives' and 'archivists'. Also the continued reference to 'records of government' implies that this discussion document (and subsequent strategy) is applicable to central government agencies only.

How do we inspire the many diverse communities in New Zealand to participate, explore and use the record of government?

Introduce the relevance and importance of records and archives in the school curriculum from year one and continue this throughout the stages of education. Children and young people should be sourcing and using records and archives as they learn and progress from primary school to high school and beyond. Educate the educators by building it into teacher training.

How can archivists help people navigate government information, records and archives in a born-digital environment?

Archives NZ needs to grow its capacity to include the expertise needed to support the preservation of born-digital information and access to it for the people of New Zealand. Empowering the regional offices to assist local populations to navigate and access digital information is pivotal. The focus of this question is somewhat misleading: helping people navigate access to information is not solely the preserve of archivists nor is the information limited to archival records.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if agencies merge or cease to exist?

Archives NZ needs to adopt a strategy that considers agencies to be interdependent rather than separate entities. The way in which we interact is now through multi-agency collaboration – everything from central government agencies sharing vital information on vulnerable children (set out in legislation) through to how we conduct our national (and international) research in teams that span organisations, sectors and nationalities. The lines are blurred in terms of being able to ascertain to which entity the information belongs, and who will be responsible for its preservation and accessibility.

Should Archives New Zealand be able to set out, more precisely, what records are to be kept for the long term because there are significant gaps (archival silences) in the record of government?

If Archives NZ does not take on this role, then who will? The archival silences would surely grow greater if there was not one body governing and setting parameters around this in a consistent manner.

Do you think Archives New Zealand should shift the focus of recordkeeping regulation to system design?

Both are important. On the latter, the crux of the strategy working effectively into the future is ‘good system design’. The majority of high value information within agencies now resides in large line-of-business systems – systems that are in the main chosen, implemented and supported by IT professionals (and related external consultants and vendors) with little or no input from other information professionals. Unless that culture is able to be changed (through regulation perhaps) then this strategy cannot be realised.

Should Archives New Zealand develop capability in-house to influence system design or work through others?

Archives NZ would ideally partner with leaders in the industry (e.g.: vendors and IT professionals). Archives NZ should also ensure that this strategy is firmly aligned with other relevant national strategies, such as the Government ICT Strategy and Action Plan. Both would allow Archives NZ to influence the development and design of the technologies that will be used to manage information in the future.

How prescriptive should Archives New Zealand be around recordkeeping standards (e.g. what should the balance be between voluntary standards versus mandatory requirements)?

The focus ought to be on a small set of clear and critical mandatory requirements so as to allow agencies to carry out their business functions whilst also ensuring that achievable compliance requirements can be met. The notion of ‘letting the system do the work’ in the strategy will only work if there are a set of clearly defined and achievable mandatory requirements. There will always be a place for both mandatory and discretionary standards, however Archives NZ will need to acknowledge that voluntary standards or ‘aspirational requirements’ are unlikely to be met in organisations with competing compliance requirements due to financial constraints.

Any other thoughts you’d like to share?

Archives NZ needs to be better resourced in order to provide access to digital information. Only a very small portion of archives have been digitised by Archives NZ and made available online, and in addition there is a significant backlog of description work to be completed. The net result of this is that a large proportion of information that resides at Archives NZ is digitally inaccessible and/or not discoverable until such time as its existence has been made known online. This does not encourage agencies to transfer information into the custody of Archives NZ.

How do we inspire the many diverse communities in New Zealand to participate, explore and use the record of government?

• By enabling people to see their history is a powerful tool. ANZ has already made a number of resources available digitally (e.g.war records) which have given people access to touch-points in their family’s or this country’s history. Fiji National Archives’ Director, Opeta Alefaio, recently gave a presentation on the issues they had faced and how they were engaging their communities with archives. They have run an extensive preservation-driven digitisation programme and this has enabled them to mount outreach programmes (roadshows) using both digital material and physical surrogates where electricity is not available. • By helping people to make links between government information and more personal dimensions of that information, as per response to the next question.

How can archivists help people navigate government information, records and archives in a born-digital environment?

• By partnering with other digital repositories to join the dots with cross-linking – for example, Auckland Museum’s Online Cenotaph is able to link to a portrait in the ANZ collections, but not to the digitised war record for the individual. A link in the opposite direction would enable people to go from the public record to an interactive space where they can add to the broader record. • By using plain language classifications to provide accessible, intelligible metadata.

What barriers exist to Māori accessing and/or managing taonga and government information for cultural and economic benefit?

• In some cases preservation storage requirements can act as a barrier. • Sometimes questions of ownership and privacy can throw up difficulties. There is no quick fix for either of these issues – ongoing dialogue is needed.

What are the pros and cons of government agencies setting their own access status classifications?

• It could be argued (in favour) that agencies need to be able to determine whether something is in such an early stage of discussion that it would be counter-productive to release information lest it generate a storm over something which is unlikely to move beyond the brainstorming stage. • It could equally be argued (against) that the public should be aware of the full context of the decision-making process • There is a danger that, left to their own devices, agencies might set their classifications in such a way as to obscure some aspects of their activities.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if government interventions are based on data?

No response

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if there are new methods of government decision-making?

• Government agencies are required to factor record-keeping and archival impacts into such changes.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if agencies merge or cease to exist?

• Keeping the historical record is negotiated between Archives NZ and the merged entity; in the event of a complete closure, ANZ takes and keeps the records.

Should Archives New Zealand be able to set out, more precisely, what records are to be kept for the long term because there are significant gaps (archival silences) in the record of government?

• Up to a point. I think we need an agreed definition of “the record of government” so that prescriptive measures are not applied too broadly. With the capacity issues identified in the document related to the expected growth in physical storage needs, perhaps it is time to review the question of what is meant by “the enduring record of government” and therefore whose records must be transferred to Archives NZ. Agencies such as universities are government-funded, but their activities are not about the operation of government, and it is in their own interest to keep an enduring record of their activities. Transfers of archives from agencies in this position have the effect of adding to ANZ’s storage burden, while removing the archives from the agency most likely to require access. Instead of a time-frame for requiring access from such agencies, and a deferral process, perhaps Archives NZ should look at requiring them to offer ANZ only such material as fits within the definition of the enduring record and which is being considered for disposal.

Do you think Archives New Zealand should shift the focus of recordkeeping regulation to system design?

I believe that quality guidance around system design would be a valuable contribution ANZ could make. Systems which are highly prescriptive are a difficult sell for RMs, but systems designed with inbuilt flexibility to be able both to respond to the particular organisation and reflect its role as an agency of government would have the potential to assist in developing better organisational practices.

Should Archives New Zealand develop capability in-house to influence system design or work through others?

I believe the expertise to assist with system design would be better developed within Archives NZ. Record keeping systems need to be developed with a proper understanding of the record keeping continuum and the knowledge-base to hear and fully understand the record keeping needs of the organisation. The danger of working through others is that the lack of that understanding will lead to “talking past each other” and build systems which do not do what they need to do.

How prescriptive should Archives New Zealand be around recordkeeping standards (e.g. what should the balance be between voluntary standards versus mandatory requirements)?

The paper notes that record-keeping maturity among public offices remains disappointing 10 years after the PRA. I believe short-term thinking in government is a significant contributor to this problem. In an environment which is constantly being re-structured, record-keeping becomes even more of a challenge than usual: it is difficult to embed a good recordkeeping culture when roles are insecure, responsibilities are moved around and little or no handover capacity is factored into changes. Organisational culture takes a very long time to change and settle and good record keeping requires more than just compliance-level commitment from senior managers. Assistance with embedding good record keeping attitudes in senior managers of government agencies would be a good starting point to developing the culture within organisations.

Any other thoughts you’d like to share?

Just to echo some of my earlier more general comments: maybe Archives NZ needs to look at the record of government like a series of concentric circles. Agencies involved in the machinery of government would sit at the centre with the highest level of prescriptiveness; agencies (such as universities) responsible to government but not a part of its operation would have the flexibility to operate within guidelines of good practice, with transfer to Archives NZ as a back-up option rather than a requirement which causes inconvenience to both parties.

How do we inspire the many diverse communities in New Zealand to participate, explore and use the record of government?

There is a case for a facilitative but passive approach to promotion. Should Archives be deciding on priorities among communities?

How can archivists help people navigate government information, records and archives in a born-digital environment?

Remote digital interactions about born-digital or digitized archives offers greater equality of access and longer reach for Archives' expert staff. Resources need to be gradually diverted from reading rooms, though some residual provision will always be necessary.

What barriers exist to Māori accessing and/or managing taonga and government information for cultural and economic benefit?

Māori face all the access barriers that other groups and individuals experience. The unique problem facing Māori is the weakness and, often, absence of fundamental descriptive and metadata layers in archive and current records. This makes identification of information of value to iwi, hapū and whanau difficult. Archives New Zealand should focus on this barrier in its own holdings and, as a regulator, in the current record of government.

What are the pros and cons of government agencies setting their own access status classifications?

It is right that the heat of accountability for access decisions is applied to government agencies. Access classifications are made by agencies in consultation with the Chief Archivist and in light of the Chief Archivist's standards. This is sufficient influence, and it could be enhanced by the routine publication of the Chief Archivist's views on access decisions.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if government interventions are based on data?

A full and accurate record of government must include visibility of the machinery of decision making, be it human or automated. The Chief Archivist's mandate to set standards can be used to specify how agencies should document their processes in an accessible and comprehensible way.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if there are new methods of government decision-making?

Accessibility by design is the goal. A mandatory PRA standard should be set for accessibility and reported on. An accessibility expectation should be built into all-of-government common capabilities. The PRA accessibility default (open and accessible at 25 years, whether in Archives or agency) should be integrated with public information about OIA, LGOIMA and Privacy Act.

How do we ensure important information remains accessible if agencies merge or cease to exist?

A comprehensive map of the information assets that make up the record of government is a necessary foundation for keeping track of maintaining that information during administrative change. This does not yet exist and should be developed and maintained. Archives should use its influence as an independent regulator and as part of DIA to ensure PRA considerations are a core component of machinery of government and legislative change guidance.

Should Archives New Zealand be able to set out, more precisely, what records are to be kept for the long term because there are significant gaps (archival silences) in the record of government?

The PRA in its current form supports wider consultation about what is kept. Archives should become more active and directive in the consultation that informs the development of retention and disposal schedules. Agencies currently often fail to identify key stakeholder groups and undertake narrow and perfunctory consultation. Consultation should be supported by a clear and, if possible, enduring acquisition policy from Archives. A supporting approach would be to develop government- or sector-wide schedules so that consultation on what is to be kept moves beyond departmental and portfolio silos.

Do you think Archives New Zealand should shift the focus of recordkeeping regulation to system design?

Yes, there should be stronger influence, indeed direction, in system design. Compulsory adoption of common capabilities and systems should be pursued, in combination with other DIA mandates, notably GCIO.

Should Archives New Zealand develop capability in-house to influence system design or work through others?

Both are necessary.

How prescriptive should Archives New Zealand be around recordkeeping standards (e.g. what should the balance be between voluntary standards versus mandatory requirements)?

A coherent and accessible record of government will not achieved without making the core requirements mandatory. A voluntary standard is just guidance.

Any other thoughts you’d like to share?

The record of government is in poor shape after a generation of rapid and haphazard shifts to digital information management. Technology, policy and process innovation has led to major improvements in government services, but has usually failed to manage the underlying information effectively. The increasing ease of creation of digital information has coincided with a period where core bureaucratic skills in managing paper information were increasingly redundant and lost, without being replaced by rules for the digital age. Similarly, long-term information preservation is seldom designed into systems. The extent of the mess is not fully known, but current indications are worrying. It may be that the best strategy has two streams. One is to leap to an improved common approach to government information management in the near future, while quarantining and preserving the existing mess for longer term clean up. Leaving the clean up to agencies will not work and, despite the unsettling cost implications, Archives should do it.
