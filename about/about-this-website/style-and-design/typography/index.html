Typefaces

We use the font Fira Sans because it:

  • is easy to read
  • is not too formal
  • matches the tone of our content
  • is open source, and
  • has good browser support — that is, it displays well in most browsers.
Example

Fira Sans

Fira Bold

Font stack

Our font stack lists all the fonts that are used, including those for people who can't use Fira Sans. They all have universal browser support and are listed in the order we would like them to appear. We chose these alternative fonts because they provide a similar experience to Fira Sans.

They are:

  • Fira Sans
  • Helvetica
  • Arial
  • Sans serif.

Spacing

We use multiples of 6 pixels (px) to create consistent spacing. Usually a gap between a heading and paragraph is 36px.

Line height

We use a line height of 1.43 times the font size. Generous line height increases white space, making the page easier to read.

Headings, summaries and paragraphs

Headings

We use headings:

  • to describe the topic or purpose of the content
  • frequently, so it's easier for users to scan a page and find the information they need
  • in the correct hierarchy — Heading 1 (H1), H2, H3 and so on — so that:
    • users can understand how each piece of content relates to the next
    • they're accessible for people using machine readers.

To meet accessibility standards, we don't:

  • use bold text instead of a heading
  • skip levels of headings
  • use headings without accompanying content.

Page titles

We give each page on the site a unique, descriptive title. They are always styled as H1.

Example

Get a Birth Certificate

Summaries

Summaries introduce the content on the page and help users make a decision about whether they’re in the right place. They appear:

  • under the page title
  • in search results — both on the site and in search engines, and
  • in menu pages.

We aim to make a summary:

  • less than 200 characters and spaces
  • include information that might answer users' needs without them reading further — sometimes called 'fail fast'
  • 1 or 2 short sentences.
Example

You can pay your income tax online if you have your e-pin.

Paragraphs — body style

Our paragraphs are:

  • left aligned — this makes the text easier to read because it provides your with eye a constant starting point for each line
  • about 80 characters and spaces in length so a users' eyes can move comfortably from 1 line to the next
  • as short as possible so they're easier to understand.

We use content elements within paragraphs to help comprehension, including bulleted and numbered lists, short sentences and note styles.

Typography — notes, alerts and warnings

We don't use:

  • italics or bold text, although there are exceptions to this
  • links in the text.

How we write

Example

In some situations you can get extra help. Call General Security at 1717. They'll assist you in all types of questions and inquiries you have.

Example

You can pay your income tax online if:

  • You are paying within the set deadlines; and
  • You already have your e-Pin.

Links

Our links are designed to be accessible and helpful to users. This includes:

  • being able to use them without relying solely on their colour
  • changing the colour of visited links
  • underlining link text that appears in body copy so that people can scan and see them
  • showing an underline when you hover over navigation links
  • identifying links to external websites.

For people using a keyboard rather than a mouse to navigate, we make it easier to use links easier by:

  • giving links a clearly visible 'focus' so users know where they are on a page
  • letting people tab between links
  • making sure links can be activated with the 'enter' key.

We have also defined how we select, name and style links.

Lists

We use lists to make it easier for people to:

  • scan the page, and
  • understand information by visually separating out the points.

Lists are always marked up with the correct html so they're accessible.

Types of lists

We use 2 types of lists. We've defined how we format and use them in content.

Style guide — lists

Unordered lists

Unordered lists are preceded by bullets.

Example

You are eligible for a driver's licence:

  • you're over 18;
  • you have a proof of residence in Lebanon; and
  • you have no criminal record.

Ordered lists

Ordered lists are preceded by numbers.

Example

There are 2 types of services:

  1. Social Services
  2. Developmental Services

Notes, alerts and warnings

  • notes have a grey sidebar
  • alerts have a blue sidebar
  • warnings have an orange sidebar.

They are used to draw attention to content in different circumstances.

Note style

Notes call attention to information that has been identified as something people often miss or misunderstand.

Example

You'll need to complete the form in one go — you can't save it and return later.

Alert style

The alert style is used to highlight information vital to the reader — often something they must do. We also use the alert style for the success message after a user submits a form.

Example

You must print out your online form before you submit it. Sign and submit it to the Ministry of Finance together with any supporting documents. Your application won't be processed until they receive the printed copy.

Warning style

Warnings are used for signalling an emergency situation that is important to a lot of people or helps people in danger. We also use the warning style for giving an error message on a form.

Example

If you or someone else is in immediate danger call 112 now.
